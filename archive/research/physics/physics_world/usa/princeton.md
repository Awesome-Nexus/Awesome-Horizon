## History

The history of physics at Princeton began in **1832** when **Joseph Henry**, a watchmaker's apprentice who became a legendary teacher and research pioneer, arrived on campus. He conducted courses in natural philosophy and engineering, and performed groundbreaking experiments in electromagnetic induction that established him at the forefront of the first golden age of science in America.

In **1873**, President James McCosh brought renowned physicist **Cyrus Fogg Brackett** to campus. Along with fellow physicist **William Magie** (Class of 1879) and mathematician **Henry B. Fine** (Class of 1880), Brackett laid a solid academic foundation that would rise to become one of the world's great centers of theoretical physics.

In the 20th century, Princeton's prominence in **relativity theory** influenced **Albert Einstein's** choice of refuge and residence, leading to his long friendship with the University.

Over the last several decades, the Department has expanded into high energy physics, condensed matter physics, mathematical physics, biological physics, nuclear physics, and astrophysics. More than **a dozen Nobel Prizes** have been awarded to faculty and students of the Department, highlighting the groundbreaking significance of their work.

## Research Areas and Specializations

The Department of Physics at Princeton conducts world-leading research across twelve major areas:

### 1. **Atomic Physics**
Research on ultracold atoms, quantum gases, atomic collisions, and precision measurement.

### 2. **Biological Physics**
Investigation of biological systems using physics principles, including molecular machines, neural networks, and collective behavior in living systems.

### 3. **Condensed Matter Experiment**
Experimental studies of quantum materials, topological phases, superconductivity, and novel electronic states.

### 4. **Condensed Matter Theory**
Theoretical work on quantum many-body systems, topological phases, strongly correlated electrons, and quantum computing materials.

### 5. **Cosmology & Gravity Theory**
Theoretical investigations of black holes, gravitational waves, early universe cosmology, and quantum gravity.

### 6. **Cosmology Experiment & Observation**
Observational cosmology including cosmic microwave background studies, large-scale structure, and dark matter/dark energy research.

### 7. **High Energy Experiment**
Participation in international collaborations at CERN's Large Hadron Collider and other particle physics facilities.

### 8. **High Energy Theory**
Research on quantum field theory, the Standard Model, supersymmetry, and physics beyond the Standard Model.

### 9. **Mathematical Physics**
Rigorous mathematical approaches to physical problems, including statistical mechanics and quantum systems.

### 10. **Particle & Nuclear Astrophysics**
Study of neutrinos, cosmic rays, and astrophysical particle phenomena.

### 11. **Particle Phenomenology**
Phenomenological analysis of particle physics data and model building.

### 12. **Plasma Physics** (through PPPL)
Research on fusion energy, plasma confinement, and basic plasma physics.

## Major Research Centers and Labs

### Princeton Plasma Physics Laboratory (PPPL)
**URL:** https://www.pppl.gov

The **Princeton Plasma Physics Laboratory** is a U.S. Department of Energy national laboratory managed by Princeton University. Founded in **1951** by Professor **Lyman Spitzer, Jr.**, PPPL is America's premier fusion research facility.

**Key Facts:**
- Over **800 scientists, engineers, and operations staff**
- Three main research focuses:
  1. Developing fusion as a clean, safe energy source
  2. Nanoscale fabrication and sustainable manufacturing
  3. Understanding the plasma universe

**Major Facilities:**
- **NSTX-U** (National Spherical Torus Experiment-Upgrade): Most powerful spherical tokamak in the world
- **LTX-Î²** (Lithium Tokamak Experiment): Testing liquid lithium wall coatings
- **FLARE** (Facility for Laboratory Reconnection Experiment): Studies magnetic reconnection

**Historical Milestones:**
- **1951**: Lyman Spitzer proposes controlled fusion research
- **1993**: TFTR achieves first magnetic fusion experiments with deuterium-tritium fuel
- **1994**: World record 10.7 million watts of fusion power produced
- **2016**: NSTX-U dedicated as most powerful spherical torus facility
- **2026**: Celebrating 75th anniversary

### Princeton Center for Theoretical Science (PCTS)
A center designed to enhance research and education in theoretical natural sciences through prestigious postdoctoral fellowships and programs exploring forefront issues in theoretical science.

### Princeton Gravity Initiative (PGI)
**URL:** https://gravity.princeton.edu

Launched in **2018**, the Princeton Gravity Initiative is a collaborative effort between Princeton's **Astrophysics, Mathematics, and Physics** departments to explore the fundamental nature of gravity.

Research areas include:
- Gravitational wave astrophysics
- Mathematical relativity
- Numerical relativity (led by Frans Pretorius)
- Early universe cosmology
- Quantum gravity
- Black hole physics

### Princeton Center for Complex Materials (PCCM)
An NSF-supported Materials Research Science and Engineering Center featuring interdisciplinary research groups studying quantum technologies and biology-inspired materials.

### Princeton Quantum Initiative
Advancing research in quantum information science, quantum computing, and quantum sensing.

### Center for the Physics of Biological Function (CPBF)
An NSF Physics Frontier Center, joint between Princeton University and CUNY Graduate Center, studying biological systems from a physics perspective.

### Institute for Research and Innovation in Software for High Energy Physics (IRIS-HEP)
An NSF-funded software institute developing cyberinfrastructure for the High Luminosity LHC and other HEP experiments.

## Graduate Program

### Program Overview
Princeton's Physics Ph.D. program is **strongly research-focused** with equal emphasis on theoretical and experimental studies. The program is designed to prepare students for careers in academia, government research laboratories, and industry.

**Key Statistics:**
- Typical class size: **20-30 students**
- Average time to Ph.D. completion: **5.4 years**
- International students: Approximately **50%** of each cohort
- Applications received annually: **600+**
- Admission offers: **50-60**

### Admission Requirements
- **Application Deadline**: December 15
- **Degree**: Ph.D. only (no separate Master's program)
- **GRE**: Optional/Not Required (General and Physics Subject tests)
- **Research Experience**: Highly valued
- **English Proficiency**: TOEFL/IELTS for non-native speakers

### Curriculum Structure

**Year 1:**
- Core courses in quantum mechanics, statistical mechanics, and electromagnetism
- Preliminary examination (January or May)
- Research exploration

**Year 2:**
- Advanced coursework in chosen specialty
- Experimental project with report and presentation
- Selection of thesis adviser
- Completion of general examination

**Years 3-5+:**
- Pre-thesis research project with oral defense
- Independent dissertation research
- Final Public Oral (FPO) examination

### Core Course Areas
1. **Quantum Mechanics/Quantum Field Theory**
   - PHY 506, 509, 510, 529

2. **Condensed Matter/Biophysics/Atomic Physics**
   - PHY 525, 526, 551, 561, 562

3. **General Relativity/High Energy Physics**
   - PHY 523, 524, 529

### Teaching Opportunities
While not required, graduate students are offered opportunities to teach at least one semester during their tenure, ranging from laboratory work to recitation sessions.

### Interdepartmental Research
Students may conduct research under supervision of faculty from:
- Astrophysical Sciences
- Plasma Physics
- Electrical Engineering
- Chemistry
- Molecular Biology
- Chemical and Biological Engineering
- Neuroscience
- Institute for Advanced Study

## Undergraduate Program

### Overview
The undergraduate physics program provides students with deep understanding of physical law and opportunities to participate in its discovery. The curriculum builds problem-solving skills, methods of estimation, and reasoning from first principles.

### Degree Requirements
- **8 departmental courses** in physics
- **2 departmental courses** in mathematics
- **Junior Papers** (2): Independent research projects
- **Senior Thesis**: Year-long research project with faculty adviser

### Recommended Course Sequence

**Freshman Year:**
| Track | Fall | Spring |
|-------|------|--------|
| Standard | PHY 103, MAT 104 | PHY 104, MAT 201 |
| Advanced | PHY 105, MAT 203 | PHY 106, MAT 204 |
| Integrated Science | ISC 231/232 | ISC 233/234 |

**Sophomore Year:**
- PHY 207 (From Classical to Quantum Mechanics) or PHY 205 (Classical Mechanics)
- PHY 208 (Principles of Quantum Mechanics)
- Additional mathematics courses

**Junior Year:**
- PHY 301 (Thermal Physics)
- PHY 304 (Advanced Electromagnetism)
- PHY 305 (Introduction to Quantum Theory)
- PHY 312 (Experimental Physics)

**Senior Year:**
- PHY 498/499 (Senior Thesis)
- Advanced electives

### Undergraduate Research
- **Summer Research**: Available for all students
- **Junior Papers**: Two independent research projects
- **Senior Thesis**: Year-long capstone research project
- **Laboratory Courses**: Hands-on experimental physics (PHY 209, 210, 312)

### Student Organizations
- **Princeton Society of Physics Students (PSPS)**: Organizes talks and activities
- **Princeton University Physics Competition (PUPC)**: High school physics competition
- **TiCuP (Towards an Inclusive Community of Undergraduate Physicists)**: Promotes inclusiveness

### Career Preparation
Physics concentrators are prepared for careers in:
- Academic and industrial physics research
- Consulting and finance
- Medicine and law
- Teaching
- Biotechnology
- University leadership
- Engineering

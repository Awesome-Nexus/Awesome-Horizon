## History

The Columbia Physics Department has one of the longest and most distinguished traditions in American physics. The graduate department was formally established in **1892**, though the roots of graduate physics can be traced to the opening of the School of Mines in **1864**.

### Key Historical Milestones

- **1895:** First physics PhD awarded to **Robert Millikan**, who later won the **1923 Nobel Prize in Physics**
- **1899:** The American Physical Society was founded at Columbia
- **1904:** The Ernest Kempton Adams (EKA) Fund established, enabling the department to invite distinguished scientists including H.A. Lorentz (1905-1906) and Max Planck (1909)
- **1925:** Pupin Laboratory completed and named after **Michael Pupin**, the central figure in the department's early years who made substantial contributions to X-ray discoveries and electromagnetism
- **1930s-40s:** The department became a hub for quantum mechanics research under **I.I. Rabi**, who won the **1944 Nobel Prize** and brought quantum mechanics knowledge to the U.S.
- **1938:** **Enrico Fermi** joined Columbia after leaving Italy, conducting pioneering work on nuclear fission that contributed to the Manhattan Project
- **1950:** **Nevis Laboratories** inaugurated with the world's most powerful cyclotron at the time
- **1967:** Columbia Astrophysics Laboratory (CAL) founded
- **1970s-present:** Continued leadership in particle physics, condensed matter, astrophysics, and nuclear physics

## Research Areas

### 1. High Energy, Nuclear, and Particle Physics

The department has a storied history in particle physics dating back to the Manhattan Project era. Current research includes:

- **ATLAS Experiment at CERN's LHC:** Design and construction of silicon pixel trackers and liquid argon calorimeters; heavy-ion physics and quark-gluon plasma studies
- **Neutrino Physics:** Experiments at Fermilab (MicroBooNE) and other facilities studying neutrino oscillations and properties
- **RHIC at Brookhaven:** Heavy ion collisions to study quark-gluon plasma
- **Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD):** Lattice QCD calculations using custom parallel computing systems

### 2. Astrophysics, Cosmology, and Gravitational Waves

**Columbia Astrophysics Laboratory (CAL):**
- Founded in 1967 with 23 faculty, 37 PhD scientists, and 40 graduate students
- X-ray astronomy and high-energy astrophysics
- Dark matter searches (XENON project)
- Cosmic microwave background studies
- VERITAS gamma-ray telescope array
- Gravitational wave detection (LIGO collaboration)
- GAPS balloon experiment in Antarctica searching for dark matter

### 3. Condensed Matter and Atomic, Molecular, and Optical (AMO) Physics

Research areas include:
- **Ultracold quantum matter:** Bose-Einstein condensates, quantum simulation
- **Low-dimensional materials:** Graphene and 2D materials (Philip Kim)
- **Quantum optics and quantum information**
- **Strongly correlated electron systems**
- **Nanoscale physics and nanoscience**
- **Superconductivity and high-temperature superconductors**

### 4. Biological and Medical Physics

- **Radiological Research Accelerator Facility (RARAF)** at Nevis: Studies of radiation effects on cells
- Biophysics of molecular machines
- Medical imaging physics

## Major Research Centers and Facilities

### On-Campus Facilities

**Pupin Hall (1925)**
- Historic home of the Physics Department
- Named after Michael Pupin
- Houses laboratories, classrooms, and administrative offices

**Schapiro Hall**
- Research laboratories and faculty offices

**Northwest Corner Building**
- Modern interdisciplinary research facility
- Advanced laboratories for physics and engineering

**Columbia Astrophysics Laboratory (CAL)**
- Collaborative research between Physics and Astronomy departments
- Located in Pupin Hall
- Focus on high-energy astrophysics and X-ray instrumentation

### Off-Campus Facilities

**Nevis Laboratories** (Irvington, New York)
- **Location:** 68-acre estate on the Hudson River, ~20 miles north of Manhattan
- **History:** Originally the Nevis estate of James Hamilton (son of Alexander Hamilton); donated to Columbia in 1934
- **Established:** 1947-1950 with construction of the synchrocyclotron

**Key Features:**
- Clean rooms for detector assembly
- State-of-the-art electronics shops
- High-performance computing cluster
- RARAF (Radiological Research Accelerator Facility)
- On-site housing for researchers
- Science-on-Hudson public lecture series

**Current Research at Nevis:**
- ATLAS detector components for CERN
- MicroBooNE neutrino experiment at Fermilab
- PHENIX experiment at RHIC
- XENON dark matter experiment
- VERITAS gamma-ray observatory
- EBEX cosmic microwave background experiment

**Radiological Research Accelerator Facility (RARAF)**
- Established in 1980
- 5.5 MV Singletron accelerator
- Unique microbeam system for single-cell irradiation
- Studies of radiation-induced cancer and bystander effects
- NIH-supported National Biomedical Technology Resource Center

## Recent Achievements and Breakthroughs (2023-2024)

### 2024 Research Highlights

1. **Bose-Einstein Condensate of Molecules**
   - Sebastian Will's lab created a unique quantum state of matter: a BEC composed of molecules
   - Published in major journals; opens path to studying new types of superfluidity

2. **Ultra-High Energy Cosmic Rays**
   - Research by Luca Comisso and Glennys Farrar showed that magnetic turbulence (not shock acceleration) is the likely source of cosmic ray energy
   - Published in *The Astrophysical Journal Letters*

3. **GAPS Balloon Experiment**
   - Successful launch in Antarctica (2024)
   - Led by Kerstin Perez; searches for dark matter using cosmic ray antiparticles

4. **LIGO Gravitational Wave Detection**
   - Columbia physicists continue leadership role in detecting and analyzing gravitational waves

### Recent Faculty Recognition

- **Brian Greene** received the 2025 AAAS Mani L. Bhaumik Award for Public Engagement with Science
- Faculty regularly publish in *Nature*, *Science*, *Physical Review Letters*, and other top journals

# Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) - Department of Physics

## Overview

The **MIT Department of Physics** is recognized as one of the best places in the world for research and education in physics. Located in Cambridge, Massachusetts, the department has consistently been ranked among the top physics departments globally, known for pioneering research across the full spectrum of physical sciences—from the smallest subatomic particles to the largest structures in the universe.

The department's philosophy centers on understanding fundamental principles governing the behavior of the physical world, including space, time, matter, and energy in all its forms—from subatomic to cosmological scales.

---

## History

MIT's Department of Physics has a storied history dating back to the early days of the Institute. The department has been at the forefront of physics research and education for over a century, contributing significantly to:

- The development of quantum mechanics and quantum field theory
- Nuclear physics and the Manhattan Project
- The discovery of quarks through electron scattering experiments
- The invention of Bose-Einstein condensation techniques
- The detection of gravitational waves
- Pioneering work in cosmology and inflation theory

The department has been the source of innovation in physics education, with **eight Oersted Medal winners**—the highest award for physics education in the United States.

### Nobel Laureates

The department boasts **five faculty members who have won the Nobel Prize in Physics**:

1. **Jerome I. Friedman** (1990) - For pioneering investigations concerning deep inelastic scattering of electrons on protons and bound neutrons, which have been of essential importance for the development of the quark model in particle physics

2. **Wolfgang Ketterle** (2001) - For the achievement of Bose-Einstein condensation in dilute gases of alkali atoms, and for early fundamental studies of the properties of the condensates

3. **Frank Wilczek** (2004) - For the discovery of asymptotic freedom in the theory of the strong interaction

4. **Samuel C.C. Ting** (1976) - For the discovery of the J/psi particle

5. **Rainer Weiss** (2017) - For decisive contributions to the LIGO detector and the observation of gravitational waves

## Research Areas

The MIT Physics Department is organized into four main research divisions:

### 1. Astrophysics

MIT astrophysicists study the largest structures in the universe, from galaxy clusters to the cosmos itself. Research areas include:

- **High-energy astrophysics**: Neutron stars, black holes, gamma-ray bursts
- **Gravitational wave astronomy**: LIGO collaboration and next-generation detectors
- **Exoplanets and planetary science**: Detection and characterization of extrasolar planets
- **Cosmology**: Dark matter, dark energy, cosmic microwave background
- **Stellar archaeology**: Ancient stars and galactic formation
- **Plasma astrophysics**: Space physics and interstellar medium

### 2. Experimental Nuclear & Particle Physics

The department conducts cutting-edge research into fundamental particles and forces:

- **Neutrino physics**: Studies of neutrino oscillations and properties
- **Dark matter detection**: Direct detection experiments
- **Heavy ion physics**: Quark-gluon plasma at RHIC and LHC
- **Particle physics at the LHC**: Higgs boson studies and beyond Standard Model searches
- **Nuclear structure**: Radioactive isotope studies

### 3. Atomic, Biophysics, Condensed Matter & Plasma Physics (ABCP)

This broad division encompasses:

- **Quantum materials**: Topological insulators, superconductors, quantum spin liquids
- **Ultracold atoms**: Bose-Einstein condensation, quantum simulation
- **Quantum information**: Quantum computing, quantum cryptography
- **Biophysics**: Molecular motors, cellular mechanics, evolutionary dynamics
- **Plasma physics**: Fusion energy, space plasma physics
- **Soft matter**: Colloids, active matter, complex fluids
- **Optical physics**: Quantum optics, precision measurement

### 4. Theoretical Nuclear & Particle Physics

Theoretical research spans:

- **Quantum field theory**: Strong and electroweak interactions
- **Lattice QCD**: Computational studies of strong interactions
- **String theory**: Quantum gravity and extra dimensions
- **Cosmological theory**: Inflation, dark energy, primordial universe
- **Condensed matter theory**: Many-body physics, topological phases

## Major Research Centers & Labs

### Affiliated Laboratories

The Physics Department is closely integrated with numerous world-class research facilities:

#### **LIGO Laboratory**
MIT is a founding member of the Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory (LIGO), which made the first direct detection of gravitational waves in 2015—confirming a major prediction of Einstein's general theory of relativity. MIT researchers continue to lead efforts in:
- Detector design and sensitivity improvements
- Data analysis and signal processing
- Next-generation gravitational wave detectors

#### **MIT Kavli Institute for Astrophysics and Space Research (MKI)**
Conducts research across astrophysics, space science, and instrumentation:
- X-ray astronomy (Chandra X-ray Observatory)
- Neutron star and black hole studies
- Space mission development

#### **Laboratory for Nuclear Science (LNS)**
The center for nuclear and particle physics at MIT:
- Hosts major experimental collaborations
- Houses computing facilities for data analysis
- Supports neutrino physics research

#### **Francis Bitter Magnet Laboratory**

- Development of high-field superconducting magnets
- Materials research in extreme magnetic fields
- Applications to condensed matter physics

#### **Plasma Science and Fusion Center (PSFC)**

## Academic Programs

### Undergraduate Program

#### Bachelor of Science in Physics (Course 8)

The undergraduate program provides a deep conceptual understanding of fundamental physics with two tracks:

**1. Focused Option**
Designed for students planning physics careers:
- Three terms of quantum mechanics (8.04, 8.05, 8.06)
- 36 units of advanced laboratory experience (8.13, 8.14)
- Required senior thesis on original research
- Comprehensive theoretical physics sequence (8.07-8.09)
- Ideal preparation for physics graduate school

**2. Flexible Option**
For students wanting strong physics foundation with flexibility:
- Core physics subjects with quantum mechanics and statistical physics
- Experimental requirement (8.13 or equivalent)
- Focus requirement: three subjects in a coherent intellectual area
- Allows for double majors or interdisciplinary study
- Excellent preparation for diverse career paths (business, law, medicine, engineering)

**Key Features:**
- **UROP (Undergraduate Research Opportunities Program)**: Students can join research labs as early as freshman year
- **TEAL (Technology-Enabled Active Learning)**: Innovative studio format for introductory physics
- Extensive elective options in astrophysics, biophysics, condensed matter, and more

#### Minors
- **Minor in Physics**: Five physics subjects beyond General Institute Requirements
- **Minor in Astronomy**: Joint with Earth, Atmospheric and Planetary Sciences

### Graduate Program

#### Doctor of Philosophy (PhD) in Physics

The doctoral program is the primary graduate program, designed to train independent researchers:

**Structure:**
- **First Year**: Coursework in core graduate subjects + 8.398 (First-Year Seminar)
- **General Examination**: Must be passed by the seventh term
- **Specialty Requirement**: Two graduate subjects in research area
- **Breadth Requirement**: Two subjects outside specialty area (can include industrial internship)
- **Thesis Research**: Original research culminating in doctoral dissertation

**Research Divisions:**
Graduate students affiliate with one of four divisions:
1. Astrophysics
2. Experimental Nuclear & Particle Physics
3. Atomic, Biophysics, Condensed Matter & Plasma Physics
4. Theoretical Nuclear & Particle Physics

**Financial Support:**
- Full financial support for all PhD students
- Teaching assistantships and research assistantships
- Fellowships available

#### Master of Science in Physics

Available only in special circumstances (e.g., military officers):
- Requires thesis representing independent research
- Typically completed in two years
- No fixed time requirement

### Special Educational Features

- **8 Oersted Medal winners** for physics education innovation
- Emphasis on combining theoretical understanding with experimental skills
- Strong computational physics training
- Opportunities for interdisciplinary research across MIT's science and engineering departments

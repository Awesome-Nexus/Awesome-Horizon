# Physics in Iceland: A Comprehensive Overview

## Executive Summary

Iceland has a small but active physics research community, with the University of Iceland serving as the primary hub for physics education and research. Given Iceland's unique geological position on the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, physics research naturally intersects with geophysics, volcanology, and renewable energy studies. The country's abundant geothermal and hydropower resources have fostered significant research in energy physics, thermodynamics, and hydrogen technology. While Iceland does not have a large dedicated physics infrastructure, its researchers actively participate in international collaborations, particularly with Nordic countries and European research organizations like CERN.

## 1. National Physics Societies

### Societas Scientiarum Islandica (Icelandic Science Society)

The **Societas Scientiarum Islandica** is Iceland's oldest scientific society, founded in 1918. While not exclusively a physics society, it serves as the primary academic body representing all natural sciences in Iceland, including physics.

**Key Activities:**
- Promotes scientific research across all disciplines in Iceland
- Publishes academic works and proceedings
- Organizes scientific meetings and conferences
- Maintains Iceland's scientific heritage and history
- Elects distinguished scientists as members

**Notable Members:**
- Jakob Yngvason (mathematical physicist)
- Thorsteinn I. Sigfusson (energy physicist)
- Various Icelandic scientists across disciplines

**Historical Significance:**
The Society has played a crucial role in establishing and maintaining Iceland's scientific community throughout the 20th century, particularly during the country's transition to independence and development of higher education.

## 2. Major University Physics Departments

### University of Iceland (Háskóli Íslands)

**Faculty of Physical Sciences (Raunvísindadeild)**

The Faculty of Physical Sciences is part of the School of Engineering and Natural Sciences (Verkfræði- og náttúruvísindasvið) and is the primary center for physics education and research in Iceland.

**Structure:**
- **Physics Division** (within the Science Institute)
- **Mathematics Division**
- **Chemistry Division**
- **Engineering Physics Program**

**Degree Programs:**

| Program | Level | Duration | Language |
|---------|-------|----------|----------|
| Physics | BS | 3 years | Icelandic |
| Engineering Physics | BS | 3 years | Icelandic |
| Physics | MS | 2 years | English/Icelandic |
| Engineering Physics | MS | 2 years | English/Icelandic |
| Physics | PhD | 3+ years | English |

**MS Specializations:**
- Theoretical Physics (offered in collaboration with Nordita)
- General Physics
- Engineering Physics

**Key Research Areas:**
- Condensed matter physics
- Thin film physics and surface science
- Hydrogen in materials
- Astrophysics and cosmology
- Quantum mechanics and quantum information theory
- Computational physics
- Geophysics

**Key Faculty and Staff:**
The Faculty of Physical Sciences had approximately 39 academic staff members as of 2020, including professors, associate professors, and research scientists.

**Facilities:**
- Science Institute (Raunvísindastofnun) - main research building
- Nanotechnology and Materials Science Centre
- Tæknigarður (Engineering Building)
- Askja - Natural Sciences Building

**Notable Features:**
- Students regularly participate in exchange programs abroad
- Strong emphasis on connecting physics education with Iceland's unique natural environment
- Close collaboration with the Nordic Institute for Theoretical Physics (Nordita)

**Location:** Reykjavík
**Website:** [https://english.hi.is/](https://english.hi.is/)

## 3. National Research Centers and Laboratories

### Icelandic Meteorological Office (IMO - Veðurstofa Íslands)

The **Icelandic Meteorological Office** is a government agency responsible for monitoring weather, climate, earthquakes, and volcanic activity in Iceland. It maintains an active research program with significant physics components.

**Key Facts:**
- **Location:** Reykjavík (Bústaðavegur 7-9)
- **Founded:** 1920
- **Employees:** ~100+ scientists and specialists
- **Annual Budget:** Government-funded

**Research Areas:**

| Area | Physics Components |
|------|-------------------|
| Meteorology | Atmospheric physics, dynamic meteorology |
| Seismology | Elastic wave propagation, geophysics |
| Volcanology | Magma physics, thermodynamics |
| Hydrology | Fluid dynamics, hydrodynamics |
| Glaciology | Ice physics, rheology |

**Physics Research Activities:**
- Physics of air-sea-land interactions
- Orographic flow modeling
- Seismic wave analysis
- Volcanic eruption dynamics
- Climate change physics

**Key Scientists:**
- Dr. Haraldur Ólafsson - Atmospheric physics, orographic flows
- Dr. Matthew J. Roberts - Multi-hazard interactions
- Various seismologists and geophysicists

**Facilities:**
- Seismic monitoring network (60+ stations)
- Weather radar systems
- Weather balloon launch facility
- Computational modeling center

**International Collaborations:**
- World Meteorological Organization (WMO)
- European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF)
- Nordic meteorological institutes

**Website:** [https://en.vedur.is/](https://en.vedur.is/)

## 4. Notable Physics Research Areas

### Renewable Energy Physics

Iceland's unique position as a world leader in renewable energy has fostered significant physics research in:

**Geothermal Energy Physics:**
- Thermodynamics of geothermal systems
- Heat transfer in porous media
- Magma-hydrothermal interactions
- Reservoir physics

**Hydropower Physics:**
- Fluid dynamics of glacial rivers
- Sediment transport physics
- Turbine efficiency optimization

**Hydrogen Economy Research:**
Iceland has been a pioneer in hydrogen energy research:
- Electrolysis technology development
- Fuel cell physics
- Hydrogen storage materials
- Integration of hydrogen with renewable energy

**Key Projects:**
- **ECTOS (Ecological City Transport System)** - hydrogen fuel cell buses
- **HYCELAND** - EU-funded Small Hydrogen Valley project
- **Icelandic New Energy** - hydrogen infrastructure development

## 6. Major Funding Bodies

### Icelandic Research Fund (Rannís - Íslensk rannsóknasjóður)

The **Icelandic Research Fund (IRF)** is the primary competitive research funding agency in Iceland, administered by Rannís (The Icelandic Centre for Research).

**Key Facts:**
- Established under Act on Public Support for Scientific Research No. 3 of 2003
- Open competitive fund for all scientific fields
- Applications must be written in English

**Grant Types:**

| Grant Type | Description | Duration |
|------------|-------------|----------|
| Grants of Excellence | Major projects with international connections | 3 years |
| Project Grants | Standard research projects | 2-3 years |
| Startup Grants | For new researchers establishing independence | 2-3 years |
| Postdoctoral Fellowships | For recent PhDs | 2-3 years |
| Doctoral Student Grants | Support for PhD students | 3 years |

**2025 Funding Statistics:**
- Total applications received: 381
- Funded applications: 64 (17%)
- New project grants: ~ISK 1.1 billion (~€7.5 million)
- Multi-year total (2025-2027): ~ISK 3.2 billion (~€22 million)

**Physics-Related Funding:**
Examples of funded physics projects include:
- "A new Physics-based Fault System and Seismic Hazard Assessment for North Iceland (NORTHAZ)" - Project grant 2025
- Various projects in geophysics, materials science, and energy physics

**Website:** [https://en.rannis.is/](https://en.rannis.is/)

## 7. International Collaborations

### CERN (European Organization for Nuclear Research)

**Iceland-CERN Relations:**

Iceland has had formal collaboration with CERN since 1996 through an International Cooperation Agreement.

**History of Collaboration:**
- **Early years:** Exchange of theoretical physicists and nuclear physicists participating through Danish and Norwegian institutions
- **1996:** Formal International Cooperation Agreement signed
- **2004:** Direct contact established with University of Iceland physicists
- **Current:** Participation in NorduGrid and ATLAS Collaboration

**Current Activities:**
- Scientists from the University of Iceland participate in **ISOLDE** experiments (Mössbauer studies of dilute semiconductors)
- Collaboration with Norwegian institutions on ISOLDE
- Icelandic students regularly participate in the **CERN Summer Student Programme**
- ATLAS collaboration activities (in conjunction with Nordic partners)

**Notable Connection:**
Icelandic physicist Ari Brynjolfsson studied at the Niels Bohr Institute (Copenhagen), which has strong CERN connections.

**Website:** [https://international-relations.web.cern.ch/stakeholder-relations/states/iceland](https://international-relations.web.cern.ch/stakeholder-relations/states/iceland)

## 8. Notable Physicists from Iceland

### Thorsteinn Ingi Sigfússon (1954–2019)

**Biography:**
- Born: June 4, 1954, in Vestmannaeyjar, Iceland
- Died: July 15, 2019
- Education: University of Copenhagen (Physics, 1978); PhD from Darwin College, Cambridge (1983)

**Career:**
- Professor of Physics at the Science Institute, University of Iceland
- Chairman of the Science Institute Board (1986–1990)
- Director of the Innovation Center Iceland
- Co-Chair of the International Partnership for the Hydrogen Economy (2003–2007)
- Created the Renewable Energy Prize Ceremony (2006)

**Research Focus:**
- Magnetism and magnetic materials
- Renewable energy systems
- Hydrogen economy
- Thermoelectrics
- Energy efficiency

**Companies Founded:**
- **Eco Energy Iceland** - renewable energy projects
- **Icelandic New Energy** - hydrogen infrastructure
- **Varmaraf** - thermoelectric devices
- **CRI (Carbon Recycling International)** - green methanol production
- **HBT** - diesel generator optimization
- **RES (Renewable Energy School)** - education in Akureyri

**Awards:**
- **Global Energy Prize (2007)** - Laureate from Russia
- Knighted by the President of Iceland (2004)
- Clerk-Maxwell award fellowship (1980)

**Legacy:**
Thorsteinn Sigfusson was Iceland's most prominent energy physicist and played a crucial role in establishing Iceland as a leader in hydrogen and renewable energy research.

### Jakob Yngvason (born 1945)

**Biography:**
- Born: November 23, 1945, in Reykjavík, Iceland
- Education: University of Göttingen (Diploma 1969, PhD 1973)
- Citizenship: Icelandic/Austrian

**Career:**
- Research Scientist, Science Institute, University of Iceland (1978–1985)
- Professor of Theoretical Physics, University of Iceland (1985–1996)
- Professor of Mathematical Physics, University of Vienna (1996–2014, now Emeritus)
- President, Erwin Schrödinger Institute for Mathematical Physics (1998–2003)
- Scientific Director, Erwin Schrödinger Institute (2004–2011)
- Vice-President, International Association of Mathematical Physics (2000–2005)

**Research Focus:**
- Mathematical physics
- Quantum field theory
- Thermodynamics and statistical mechanics
- Bose-Einstein condensation
- Quantum many-body systems
- Cold atomic gases
- Adiabatic accessibility

**Major Contributions:**
- Co-authored definitive monograph: *"The Mathematics of the Bose Gas and its Condensation"* (2005) with Elliott H. Lieb, Robert Seiringer, and Jan Philip Solovej
- Work with Elliott Lieb on mathematical explanation of the second law of thermodynamics
- Contributions to local quantum field theory

**Awards:**
- **Levi L. Conant Prize (2002)** - American Mathematical Society (shared with Elliott H. Lieb)
- **Erwin Schrödinger Prize (2004)** - Austrian Academy of Sciences

**Memberships:**
- Societas Scientiarum Islandica
- Corresponding member, Academy of Sciences in Göttingen
- Corresponding member, Royal Danish Academy of Science and Letters

**Legacy:**
Jakob Yngvason is Iceland's most internationally recognized theoretical physicist, with significant contributions to the mathematical foundations of thermodynamics and quantum statistical mechanics.

### Ari Brynjólfsson (1926–2013)

**Biography:**
- Born: December 7, 1926, in Akureyri, Iceland
- Died: June 28, 2013, in Tampa, Florida, USA
- Education: Menntaskólinn á Akureyri (1948); Niels Bohr Institute, University of Copenhagen (PhD 1954)

**Career:**
- Special Research Fellow, University of Iceland (1954–1955)
- Alexander von Humboldt Fellow, University of Göttingen (1955–1957)
- Head of Radiation Facilities, Risø Laboratory, Denmark (1957–1965)
- Head of US Army Radiation Facilities, Natick, Massachusetts (1965–1980)
- Director, IFFIT (International Facility for Food Irradiation Technology), FAO/IAEA (1988–1992)

**Research Focus:**
- Nuclear physics
- Radiation physics
- Food irradiation technology
- Plasma redshift theory
- Magnetism in rocks
- Magnetic field reversals

**Major Contributions:**
- Pioneer in food irradiation technology for food safety
- Developed radiation facilities worldwide
- Research on plasma redshift and alternative cosmological theories
- Early work on magnetic field reversals using self-devised instruments

**Awards:**
- Møller Foundation Award for exceptional service to Danish Industry (1965)
- Radiation Science and Technology Award, American Nuclear Society (1988)
- US Brigadier General Merrill L. Tribe Award (1963)

**Legacy:**
Ari Brynjolfsson was a prominent Icelandic-American nuclear physicist who made significant contributions to food safety through radiation technology, though he later became known for controversial alternative cosmological theories.

## 10. Summary and Future Outlook

Iceland's physics landscape is characterized by:

### Strengths:
1. **Unique Natural Laboratory:** Iceland's geology provides unparalleled opportunities for geophysics, volcanology, and geothermal research
2. **Renewable Energy Leadership:** World-leading expertise in geothermal and hydropower physics
3. **International Integration:** Strong connections to Nordic and European research networks
4. **Quality over Quantity:** Small but high-quality research output per capita
5. **Energy Innovation:** Pioneer in hydrogen economy research

### Challenges:
1. **Small Population Base:** Limited number of physicists and students
2. **Limited Infrastructure:** No major international physics facilities
3. **Geographic Isolation:** Distance from major European research centers
4. **Language Barrier:** Icelandic language limits some international activities

### Emerging Opportunities:
- **Green Energy Transition:** Expanding research in hydrogen, carbon capture, and sustainable technologies
- **Climate Research:** Increasing focus on climate physics and impacts
- **Quantum Technologies:** Growing interest in quantum information science
- **Nordic Collaborations:** Strengthening ties with Nordic research institutions
- **European Research Programs:** Increased participation in Horizon Europe

### Key Statistics:
- **Universities with Physics Programs:** 1 major (University of Iceland)
- **Physics Faculty:** ~15-20 permanent positions
- **Physics PhDs per Year:** ~2-5
- **International Collaborations:** Active with CERN, Nordita, and European programs

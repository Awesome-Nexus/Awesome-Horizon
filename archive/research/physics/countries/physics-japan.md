# Physics in Japan: A Comprehensive Overview

Japan is one of the world's leading nations in physics research, with a rich history of scientific excellence, numerous Nobel Prize laureates, and cutting-edge research facilities. This document provides a comprehensive overview of physics institutions, societies, facilities, and notable physicists in Japan.

---

## 1. National Physics Societies

### Physical Society of Japan (JPS)
- **Founded**: 1946 (originally established in 1877 as the Tokyo Academy of Sciences)
- **Members**: Over 20,000 physicists
- **Publications**: 
  - *Journal of the Physical Society of Japan* (JPSJ) - primary research journal
  - *Progress of Theoretical and Experimental Physics* (PTEP) - open-access journal
- **Activities**: Annual meetings, specialized divisions covering all areas of physics, awards and prizes
- **Website**: https://www.jps.or.jp/english/

### Japan Society of Applied Physics (JSAP)
- **Founded**: 1932
- **Members**: Over 30,000 researchers
- **Focus**: Applied physics research and industrial applications
- **Publications**: *Japanese Journal of Applied Physics*

### Astronomical Society of Japan
- Promotes astronomy and astrophysics research
- Publishes *Publications of the Astronomical Society of Japan*

## 3. National Research Centers

### RIKEN
**The Institute of Physical and Chemical Research**

- **Founded**: 1917
- **Status**: National research institute
- **Location**: Wako, Saitama (headquarters); multiple campuses
- **Staff**: Over 3,000 researchers

**RIKEN Nishina Center for Accelerator-Based Science**
- Focuses on nuclear physics, heavy ion physics
- Operates heavy ion accelerators
- RHIC Physics Research Laboratory (collaboration with Brookhaven)

**RIKEN Center for Quantum Computing (RQC)**
- Research on quantum computing technologies

**RIKEN iTHEMS (Interdisciplinary Theoretical and Mathematical Sciences Program)**
- Brings together researchers from physics, mathematics, and biology
- Focuses on interdisciplinary research at the forefront of theoretical sciences

**RIKEN Center for Computational Science (R-CCS)**
- Home to Fugaku supercomputer (one of the world's fastest)
- Quantum chromodynamics simulations
- Materials science computations

**Website**: https://www.riken.jp/en/

### KEK (High Energy Accelerator Research Organization)

- **Founded**: 1997 (merger of previous laboratories)
- **Location**: Tsukuba and Tokai, Ibaraki Prefecture
- **Status**: Inter-University Research Institute

**Major Laboratories**:
1. **Accelerator Laboratory** - Studies, develops, builds and manages particle accelerators
2. **Institute of Particle and Nuclear Studies (IPNS)** - Fundamental physics research
3. **Institute of Materials Structure Science (IMSS)** - Materials research using synchrotron radiation, neutrons, and muons
4. **Applied Research Laboratory** - Support for accelerator-based research

**Current Accelerators and Facilities**:
- **SuperKEKB**: Electron-positron collider (upgrade of KEKB), operating since 2018
- **Photon Factory (PF)**: 2.5 GeV synchrotron light source
- **Photon Factory Advanced Ring (PF-AR)**: 6.5 GeV synchrotron light source
- **J-PARC**: Japan Proton Accelerator Research Complex (joint with JAEA)
- **Accelerator Test Facility (ATF)**: Testbed for future linear colliders
- **Superconducting RF Test Facility (STF)**: Testing for International Linear Collider

**Website**: https://www.kek.jp/en/

### NIFS (National Institute for Fusion Science)

- **Location**: Toki City, Gifu Prefecture
- **Status**: National Institute of Natural Sciences
- **Focus**: Fusion energy research and plasma physics

**Major Facilities**:
- **LHD (Large Helical Device)**: 
  - World's largest superconducting helical fusion device
  - Major radius: 3.9 m
  - Magnetic field: Up to 3 T
  - Purpose: Steady-state fusion plasma research

**Research Areas**:
- Plasma confinement and stability
- Heating and current drive
- Plasma-material interactions
- Divertor physics
- Superconducting magnet technology

**Website**: https://www.nifs.ac.jp/

### QST (National Institutes for Quantum Science and Technology)

- **Formed**: 2016 (merger of previous organizations)
- **Focus**: Quantum science, radiation science, fusion energy

**Major Responsibilities**:
- Japan's contribution to ITER project
- Broader Approach Agreement with EU
- Medical applications of radiation
- Quantum technologies

**Website**: https://www.qst.go.jp/

### NAOJ (National Astronomical Observatory of Japan)

- **Founded**: 1988
- **Part of**: National Institutes of Natural Sciences (NINS)

**Major Facilities**:
- **Subaru Telescope**: 8.2m optical-infrared telescope at Mauna Kea, Hawaii
- **KAGRA**: Gravitational wave detector at Kamioka (3km interferometer)
- **Nobeyama Radio Observatory**: 45m radio telescope, solar radio observations
- **VERA (VLBI Exploration of Radio Astrometry)**: Network of radio telescopes for astrometry
- **ALMA**: Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (international collaboration)

**Website**: https://www.nao.ac.jp/en/

### JAEA (Japan Atomic Energy Agency)

- Nuclear energy research
- Nuclear safety
- Fusion research (collaboration with NIFS and QST)

## 4. Major Research Facilities

### SuperKEKB and Belle II

**Location**: KEK, Tsukuba

**SuperKEKB**:
- Electron-positron collider
- Upgrade of previous KEKB B-factory
- Target luminosity: 8×10³⁵ cm⁻² s⁻¹ (40× higher than KEKB)
- Uses nano-beam scheme for beam focusing

**Belle II Experiment**:
- Detector for studying B-meson physics
- Searches for new physics beyond the Standard Model
- Studies CP violation and rare decays
- Began operation in 2019
- Collaboration of over 1,000 researchers from 26 countries

### Super-Kamiokande

**Location**: Kamioka Mine, Hida City, Gifu Prefecture
**Operated by**: ICRR, University of Tokyo

**Detector Specifications**:
- Type: Water Cherenkov detector
- Mass: 50,000 tonnes of ultrapure water
- Depth: 1,000 meters underground
- PMTs: 11,146 photomultiplier tubes (50cm diameter) in inner detector

**Major Discoveries**:
- First evidence of neutrino oscillation (1998) - Nobel Prize 2015
- Atmospheric neutrino observations
- Solar neutrino measurements
- Neutrinos from supernova SN1987A

**Current Status**: Active; undergoing upgrades including SK-Gd (gadolinium addition)

**Website**: https://www-sk.icrr.u-tokyo.ac.jp/en/sk/

### Hyper-Kamiokande

**Status**: Under construction; data-taking planned for 2028

**Location**: 
- Far detector: Tochibora mine, Hida City (8km from Super-Kamiokande)
- Near detectors: J-PARC, Tokai

**Specifications**:
- Water Cherenkov detector, 258 kilotons of water
- 5× larger than Super-Kamiokande
- Cylindrical tank: 68m diameter × 71m height
- 20,000+ PMTs planned

**Science Goals**:
- CP violation in neutrino oscillations
- Proton decay search (target sensitivity: 10³⁵ years)
- Supernova neutrino detection
- Atmospheric neutrino studies
- Dark matter searches

**Website**: https://www-sk.icrr.u-tokyo.ac.jp/en/hk/

### J-PARC (Japan Proton Accelerator Research Complex)

**Location**: Tokai, Ibaraki Prefecture
**Operated by**: KEK and JAEA

**Facilities**:
- **400 MeV Linac**: Proton linear accelerator
- **3 GeV Rapid Cycling Synchrotron**: High-intensity proton beam
- **50 GeV Main Ring**: High-energy proton synchrotron
- **Materials and Life Science Experimental Facility (MLF)**: Neutron and muon beams
- **Hadron Experimental Facility**: Hadron physics
- **Neutrino Facility**: Produces beam for T2K and Hyper-Kamiokande

**Research Areas**:
- Particle physics (neutrino oscillations)
- Nuclear physics
- Hadron physics
- Materials science with neutrons
- Muon science
- Accelerator-driven transmutation

**Website**: https://j-parc.jp/

### LHD (Large Helical Device)

**Location**: National Institute for Fusion Science, Toki City, Gifu

**Specifications**:
- Type: Helical fusion device (stellarator concept)
- Major radius: 3.9 m
- Minor radius: ~0.6 m
- Magnetic field: Up to 3 Tesla
- Plasma volume: ~30 m³
- Superconducting helical coils

**Research Focus**:
- Steady-state high-temperature plasma confinement
- Achieved ion temperatures over 100 million degrees
- Long-pulse operation (up to 1 hour)

### KAGRA

**Location**: Kamioka Mine, Hida City, Gifu Prefecture
**Operated by**: ICRR (University of Tokyo), NAOJ, KEK

**Specifications**:
- Type: Cryogenic laser interferometer gravitational wave detector
- Arm length: 3 km
- Location: Underground (200-450m depth) for seismic noise reduction
- Mirror temperature: -250°C (cryogenic operation)
- Mirror material: Sapphire

**Status**: Active observation runs in collaboration with LIGO and Virgo (LVK collaboration)

**Website**: https://gwcenter.icrr.u-tokyo.ac.jp/en/

### T2K Experiment

**Neutrino Beam**: J-PARC to Super-Kamiokande
**Distance**: 295 km
**Off-axis angle**: 2.5°

**Discoveries**:
- First observation of electron neutrino appearance from muon neutrino beam (2013)
- First indication of CP violation in neutrino sector
- Precise measurement of neutrino oscillation parameters

**Status**: Active; continues operation until Hyper-Kamiokande takes over

### Subaru Telescope

**Location**: Mauna Kea, Hawaii, USA
**Operated by**: NAOJ

**Specifications**:
- Type: Optical-infrared telescope
- Primary mirror: 8.2 meters (monolithic)
- Altitude: 4,139 meters
- Prime focus capability: Unique among 8m-class telescopes

**Instruments**:
- Hyper Suprime-Cam (HSC): Wide-field imaging camera
- Prime Focus Spectrograph (PFS): Multi-object spectrograph
- IRCS: Infrared camera and spectrograph
- Various other instruments for visible and IR observations

**Website**: https://www.nao.ac.jp/en/research/telescope/subaru.html

### Photon Factory (KEK)

**Specifications**:
- Electron storage ring: 2.5 GeV
- Circumference: 187 m
- Purpose: Synchrotron radiation research

**Applications**:
- Materials structure analysis
- Protein crystallography
- X-ray absorption spectroscopy
- Photoelectron spectroscopy

## 5. Notable Physicists and Nobel Laureates

### Nobel Prize in Physics Laureates from Japan

#### Hideki Yukawa (1949)
- **Awarded for**: Prediction of the existence of mesons based on theoretical work on nuclear forces
- **Born**: 1907, Tokyo
- **Institution**: Kyoto University
- **Significance**: First Japanese Nobel laureate; established meson theory explaining the strong nuclear force
- **Legacy**: Yukawa Institute for Theoretical Physics named in his honor

#### Shin'ichirō Tomonaga (1965)
- **Awarded for**: Fundamental work in quantum electrodynamics (QED)
- **Born**: 1906, Kyoto
- **Institution**: Tokyo University of Education (now University of Tsukuba)
- **Shared with**: Richard Feynman and Julian Schwinger
- **Contribution**: Developed the renormalization method for QED; discovered "super-many-time theory"
- **Notable**: Classmate of Hideki Yukawa at Kyoto University

#### Leo Esaki (1973)
- **Awarded for**: Experimental discoveries regarding tunneling phenomena in semiconductors
- **Born**: 1925, Osaka
- **Institution**: IBM Thomas J. Watson Research Center (previously Sony Corporation)
- **Shared with**: Ivar Giaever and Brian Josephson
- **Invention**: Tunnel diode (Esaki diode) - first quantum electron device
- **Discovery made while**: Working at Tokyo Tsushin Kogyo (now Sony) in 1957

#### Masatoshi Koshiba (2002)
- **Awarded for**: Pioneering contributions to astrophysics, particularly for the detection of cosmic neutrinos
- **Born**: 1926, Toyohashi
- **Institution**: University of Tokyo
- **Shared with**: Raymond Davis Jr. and Riccardo Giacconi
- **Contributions**: 
  - Built Kamiokande detector
  - First detection of neutrinos from supernova SN1987A
  - Confirmed solar neutrino problem

#### Makoto Kobayashi (2008)
- **Awarded for**: Discovery of the origin of the broken symmetry which predicts the existence of at least three families of quarks
- **Born**: 1944, Nagoya
- **Institution**: KEK (High Energy Accelerator Research Organization)
- **Shared with**: Yoichiro Nambu and Toshihide Maskawa
- **Contribution**: Kobayashi-Maskawa matrix (CKM matrix) describing quark mixing

#### Toshihide Maskawa (2008)
- **Awarded for**: Discovery of the origin of the broken symmetry which predicts the existence of at least three families of quarks
- **Born**: 1940, Nagoya
- **Institution**: Kyoto Sangyo University; former Director of Yukawa Institute
- **Contribution**: CKM matrix with Kobayashi; predicted three generations of quarks before discovery

#### Yoichiro Nambu (2008)
- **Awarded for**: Discovery of the mechanism of spontaneous broken symmetry in subatomic physics
- **Born**: 1921, Tokyo (Japanese-American)
- **Institution**: University of Chicago
- **Contribution**: 
  - Spontaneous symmetry breaking in particle physics (1960)
  - Nambu-Goldstone boson concept
  - Color charge concept in QCD
  - Influenced Higgs mechanism development

#### Isamu Akasaki (2014)
- **Awarded for**: Invention of efficient blue light-emitting diodes
- **Born**: 1929, Kagoshima
- **Institution**: Nagoya University / Meijo University
- **Shared with**: Hiroshi Amano and Shuji Nakamura

#### Hiroshi Amano (2014)
- **Awarded for**: Invention of efficient blue light-emitting diodes
- **Born**: 1960, Hamamatsu
- **Institution**: Nagoya University
- **Shared with**: Isamu Akasaki and Shuji Nakamura

#### Takaaki Kajita (2015)
- **Awarded for**: Discovery of neutrino oscillations, which shows that neutrinos have mass
- **Born**: 1959, Higashimatsuyama
- **Institution**: University of Tokyo (ICRR)
- **Shared with**: Arthur B. McDonald
- **Discovery**: Atmospheric neutrino oscillation at Super-Kamiokande (1998)
- **Current role**: Director of ICRR

### Other Notable Japanese Physicists

#### Yoshio Nishina (1890-1951)
- Father of modern physics in Japan
- Established RIKEN's nuclear physics research
- Trained many leading Japanese physicists including Tomonaga
- Nishina Center at RIKEN named after him

#### Kenjiro Takayanagi (1899-1990)
- Pioneer of television technology
- Developed high-definition television (HDTV)

#### Sin-Itiro Tomonaga's Students
- Many became leaders in Japanese physics
- Established strong tradition in theoretical physics

## 6. Physics Research Areas of Strength

### Neutrino Physics
Japan is a world leader in neutrino research:
- **Neutrino oscillation**: Discovery by Super-Kamiokande (1998)
- **Long-baseline experiments**: T2K, future Hyper-Kamiokande
- **Solar neutrinos**: Kamiokande, Super-Kamiokande
- **Supernova neutrinos**: Detection of SN1987A
- **Proton decay**: Search with large water Cherenkov detectors

### High Energy / Particle Physics
- **B-physics**: Belle and Belle II experiments at KEK
- **Neutrino physics**: K2K, T2K, Hyper-Kamiokande
- **CP violation**: Kobayashi-Maskawa theory
- **Hadron physics**: J-PARC hadron facility
- **Future**: International Linear Collider (proposed)

### Condensed Matter Physics
Strong traditions in:
- **Superconductivity**: Discovery of various cuprate and iron-based superconductors
- **Magnetism**: Spintronics research, quantum spin liquids
- **Topological materials**: Topological insulators, Weyl semimetals
- **Strongly correlated electrons**: Quantum critical phenomena
- **Low temperature physics**: Ultra-low temperature techniques

### Fusion and Plasma Physics
- **Stellarator research**: LHD facility (world-leading)
- **Tokamak research**: JT-60SA (collaboration with EU)
- **ITER participation**: Japan is a key member nation
- **Plasma-material interactions**: Advanced research

### Gravitational Wave Astronomy
- **KAGRA**: First underground cryogenic gravitational wave detector
- **International collaboration**: LIGO-Virgo-KAGRA network
- **Multi-messenger astronomy**: Coordination with Subaru telescope

### Quantum Science and Technology
- **Quantum computing**: RIKEN and university research
- **Quantum information**: Multiple research centers
- **Quantum materials**: Topological quantum computing research
- **Quantum optics**: Ultra-cold atom research

### Nuclear Physics
- **RIKEN Nishina Center**: Heavy ion physics, RI Beam Factory
- **J-PARC**: Hadron physics, nuclear astrophysics
- **Theoretical nuclear physics**: YITP, various universities

### Astrophysics and Cosmology
- **Subaru Telescope**: Wide-field surveys, galaxy formation
- **KAGRA**: Gravitational wave astronomy
- **VERA**: Radio astrometry
- **ALMA**: Submillimeter astronomy (Japanese participation)

### Accelerator Science
- **KEK**: Leading accelerator laboratory in Asia
- **SuperKEKB**: Highest luminosity collider
- **J-PARC**: High-intensity proton accelerator
- **Future**: ILC design and site consideration in Japan

### Materials Science with Neutrons and Muons
- **J-PARC MLF**: World's most powerful pulsed neutron source
- **KEK IMSS**: Synchrotron radiation, muon science

## 7. International Collaborations

### CERN Collaboration
Japan has a strong partnership with CERN:
- **ATLAS and CMS experiments**: Major Japanese contributions to detectors and physics analysis
- **LHC upgrades**: Japanese technology contributions
- **Japanese researchers**: Hundreds of scientists from Japanese universities and KEK
- **KEK-CERN cooperation**: Technical collaborations, personnel exchange

### ITER (International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor)
Japan is one of seven ITER member parties:
- **Contribution**: 9.1% of construction costs
- **Organization**: QST implements Japan's contribution
- **Personnel**: Japanese engineers and scientists at ITER site
- **Domestic Agency**: Japan Atomic Energy Agency (procurement)

### Broader Approach Agreement (EU-Japan)
Complementary to ITER, three major projects in Japan:

#### JT-60SA (Naka, Ibaraki)
- **Type**: Superconducting tokamak
- **Status**: Operating since October 2023
- **Size**: World's largest operating tokamak (similar scale to ITER)
- **Purpose**: Support ITER operation, DEMO preparation
- **Partnership**: EU-Japan collaboration

#### IFMIF/EVEDA (Rokkasho, Aomori)
- **Type**: Materials irradiation facility
- **Purpose**: Test materials for fusion reactors

#### IFERC (Rokkasho, Aomori)
- **International Fusion Energy Research Centre**
- **Components**:
  - Supercomputer for fusion simulation
  - Remote experimentation room for ITER
  - DEMO design activities

### LIGO-Virgo-KAGRA Collaboration
- **KAGRA**: Japan's gravitational wave detector
- **Network**: Works with LIGO (USA) and Virgo (Italy)
- **Joint observations**: Coordinated gravitational wave detection runs
- **Multi-messenger astronomy**: Combined with electromagnetic observations

### Belle II International Collaboration
- **Members**: 26 countries, 1000+ researchers
- **Participation**: International contributions to detector and physics

### Hyper-Kamiokande Collaboration
- **Members**: Over 20 countries across six continents
- **Status**: International collaboration forming

### ALMA (Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array)
- **Partners**: ESO, NAOJ, NRAO
- **Japanese contribution**: Antennas, receivers, operations
- **East Asian participation**: Japan leads East Asian contribution

### International Space Station
- **Kibo module**: Japanese experiment module
- **Physics experiments**: Various fundamental physics investigations

### X-Ray Free Electron Laser
- **SACLA**: Japanese XFEL at SPring-8
- **International users**: Open to worldwide researchers

### Neutrino Experiments
- **T2K**: Japan hosts, international collaboration
- **Hyper-Kamiokande**: Japan leads with international partners
- **NOvA**: US-Japan collaboration (joint analysis with T2K)

### Future: International Linear Collider (ILC)
- **Proposal**: Electron-positron linear collider
- **Site candidate**: Tohoku region, Japan
- **Status**: Technical Design Report completed; awaiting political decisions
- **Japanese interest**: Strong support from Japanese physics community

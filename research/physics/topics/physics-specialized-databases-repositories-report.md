## PARTICLE PHYSICS

### 1. INSPIRE-HEP (High Energy Physics)

**URL:** https://inspirehep.net/

**Description:**
INSPIRE-HEP is the premier information system for high energy physics (HEP), jointly developed and maintained by CERN, DESY, Fermilab, and SLAC. It serves as the definitive source for bibliographic information in particle physics and related fields.

**Key Features:**
- **Comprehensive Coverage:** Over 1.5 million bibliographic records including articles, preprints, reports, and conference proceedings
- **Author Profiles:** Detailed author disambiguation with publication histories and citation metrics
- **Citation Analysis:** Full citation network analysis with h-index calculation
- **Job Listings:** Integrated HEPJobs database for academic positions
- **Conference Database:** Complete database of HEP conferences and workshops
- **Experiment Records:** Information about past and present HEP experiments
- **Literature Suggestions:** AI-powered paper recommendations based on reading history
- **API Access:** REST API for programmatic access to all data
- **LaTeX/BibTeX Export:** Direct export of citations in multiple formats
- **ORCID Integration:** Automatic linking with ORCID profiles

**Search Capabilities:**
- Advanced search with Boolean operators
- Full-text search across abstracts and titles
- Author affiliation tracking
- Date range filtering
- Document type filtering (article, thesis, report, etc.)
- Citation count sorting
- Customizable result views

**Sub-Databases:**
- HEPNames: Author database with affiliation history
- HEPInstitutes: Institution database
- HEPJobs: Academic job postings
- HEPConferences: Conference database
- HEPExperiments: Experiment database

**Collections:**

## CONCLUSION

This comprehensive research report covers the major specialized databases, repositories, and tools available to physics researchers. These resources span:

1. **Discipline-Specific Databases:** Tailored to subfields like particle physics, astrophysics, condensed matter, and nuclear physics
2. **Data Repositories:** For storing and sharing research data, with DOI assignment and long-term preservation
3. **Citation Databases:** For tracking research impact and discovering related work
4. **Open Access Repositories:** For rapid dissemination of preprints and publications
5. **Historical Archives:** Preserving the documentary heritage of physics

Researchers should select resources based on their specific needs, considering factors such as:
- Discipline relevance
- Data type requirements
- Access restrictions (open vs. subscription)
- Integration with existing workflows
- Long-term preservation guarantees
- Citation and discoverability features

The landscape of physics information resources continues to evolve, with trends toward:
- Increased open access
- Better data interoperability
- FAIR data principles adoption
- Machine-readable metadata
- API-first architectures
- AI-powered discovery tools

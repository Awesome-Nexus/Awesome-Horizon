# Physics in Brazil: A Comprehensive Overview

Brazil stands as the leading physics nation in Latin America, with a rich scientific tradition dating back to the 1930s. The country has developed a robust ecosystem of research institutions, universities, and international collaborations that have produced world-class physics research and notable scientific discoveries.

## 1. National Physics Societies

### Sociedade Brasileira de Física (SBF) - Brazilian Physical Society

**Founded:** July 14, 1966 (during the XVIII Annual Meeting of the SBPC in Blumenau, Santa Catarina)

**Founding President:** Oscar Sala

**Headquarters:** São Paulo, Brazil

**Members:** Over 5,000 members across three categories:
- Aspiring members (undergraduate students)
- Regular members (professionals)
- Effective members (doctoral degree holders)

**Mission:**
- Promote advancement and dissemination of physics knowledge
- Defend academic freedom and interests of physics professionals
- Contribute to public policies improving physics education and research
- Support scientific and technological development of Brazil

**Publications:**
1. **Brazilian Journal of Physics** (since 1971) - International peer-reviewed research journal
2. **Revista Brasileira de Ensino de Física** (since 1979) - Open access journal focused on physics teaching (Portuguese)
3. **A Física na Escola** (since 2000) - Journal for physics teaching at all levels (Portuguese)
4. **Electronic weekly Bulletin** - Physics news

**Divisions (12 specialized areas):**
1. Atomic & Molecular Physics
2. Biological Physics
3. Statistical Physics
4. Industrial Physics
5. Mathematical Physics
6. Condensed Matter Physics
7. Medical Physics
8. Optics and Photonics
9. Research in Physics Education
10. Plasma Physics
11. Particles and Fields
12. Nuclear Physics

**Major Activities:**
- Organizes national meetings in all fields of physics (annual/biennial)
- Coordinates the **Professional Master's Degree in Physics Education** (MNPEF) since 2014
- Organizes the **Brazilian Physics Olympiad (OBF)** and **Brazilian Physics Olympiad in Public Schools (OBFEP)** - involving ~265,000 students annually

**Annual Prizes:**
- **José Leite Lopes Prize** - Best PhD Thesis
- **Carolina Nemes Prize** - Outstanding women in early career stages
- **Joaquim da Costa Ribeiro Prize** - Career contributions in condensed matter physics and materials sciences
- **Ernesto Hamburger Prize** - Popularization and dissemination of physics
- **Anselmo Salles Paschoa Prize** - Excellence in research for Afro-Brazilian physicists

## 2. Major University Physics Departments

### 2.1 University of São Paulo (USP)

**Institute of Physics (IFUSP)**

**Founded:** 1934 (as part of the Faculty of Philosophy, Sciences and Letters), became independent Institute in 1970

**Location:** São Paulo, SP (main campus)

**History:**
The Department of Physics was established in 1934 with the founding of USP, under the leadership of Italian physicist **Gleb Wataghin** and **Giuseppe Occhialini**. This marked the beginning of modern physics research in Brazil. The Institute of Physics became autonomous from the former FFCL (School of Philosophy, Sciences and Letters) in 1970.

**Notable Features:**
- One of Brazil's largest and most prestigious physics institutions
- Built Brazil's first tokamak (TBR-1) in 1981 - the first in Latin America
- Houses the Plasma Physics Laboratory with the TCABR tokamak
- Strong traditions in nuclear physics, condensed matter, and plasma physics

**Research Areas:**
- Condensed Matter Physics
- Nuclear Physics
- Plasma Physics and Fusion
- Elementary Particle Physics
- Statistical Physics
- Applied Physics

**Facilities:**
- Van de Graaff accelerator
- Pelletron electrostatic accelerator (first in Latin America)
- Plasma Physics Laboratory (TCABR tokamak)
- Advanced materials characterization labs

### 2.2 State University of Campinas (Unicamp)

**Institute of Physics "Gleb Wataghin" (IFGW)**

**Founded:** 1966 (Institute established), named after Gleb Wataghin in 1986

**Location:** Campinas, SP (Barão Geraldo campus)

**History:**
Unicamp was designed from scratch as an integrated research center, unlike other Brazilian universities created from existing schools. The Institute of Physics was established with the founding of the university and later named after Gleb Wataghin to honor his contributions to Brazilian physics.

**Key Features:**
- Close proximity to the Brazilian Synchrotron Light Laboratory (LNLS)
- Strong collaboration with CNPEM
- Home to the César Lattes Central Library
- ~15% of Brazilian research output

**Research Areas:**
- Condensed Matter Physics
- Cosmic Rays and High Energy Physics
- General Relativity and Gravitation
- Statistical Physics
- Optics and Photonics
- Materials Science

**Notable Achievements:**
- Lattes' work on cosmic rays and "fireballs"
- Strong experimental physics programs
- Advanced materials research

### 2.3 Federal University of Rio de Janeiro (UFRJ)

**Institute of Physics (IF-UFRJ)**

**Founded:** 1964

**Location:** Rio de Janeiro, RJ (Cidade Universitária, Ilha do Fundão)

**History:**
The Institute of Physics was created in 1964, consolidating physics research at what was then the University of Brazil. The university itself traces its origins to 1792 (Royal Academy of Artillery, Fortification and Design).

**Undergraduate Programs:**

**Graduate Programs:**

- Applied Physics
- Biophysics

**Notable Labs:**

- Nuclear Physics Laboratory
- Computational Physics Laboratory

## 3. National Research Centers

### 3.1 Centro Brasileiro de Pesquisas Físicas (CBPF)
**Brazilian Center for Research in Physics**

**Founded:** January 1949

**Founders:** César Lattes, José Leite Lopes, and Jayme Tiomno

**Location:** Urca neighborhood, Rio de Janeiro, RJ

**Sponsoring Agency:** CNPq (National Council for Scientific and Technological Development)

**History:**
CBPF was established as the first theoretical physics research center in Latin America, initially funded by the Brazilian Confederation of Industries. Throughout its history, CBPF became internationally renowned and hosted many world-famous physicists including **Richard Feynman** and **J. Robert Oppenheimer**.

**Spun-off Institutions:**
- IMPA (National Institute for Pure and Applied Mathematics, 1952)
- LNCC (National Laboratory for Scientific Computation)
- LNLS (National Synchrotron Light Laboratory)

**Current Research Areas:**
- Experimental Low-Energy Physics
- Cosmology, Relativity and Astrophysics
- Applied Physics
- Experimental High-Energy Physics
- Mathematical Physics
- Nanophysics
- Biological Physics

**Notable Directors:**
- Ricardo Galvão (2004-2011)
- José Leite Lopes (returned as director in 1986 after exile)

---

### 3.2 Instituto Nacional de Pesquisas Espaciais (INPE)
**National Institute for Space Research**

**Founded:** April 22, 1971

**Location:** São José dos Campos, SP

**Parent Organization:** Ministry of Science, Technology and Innovations (MCTI)

**History:**
Originated from COGNAE (Organizing Group for the National Commission on Space Activities) created in 1961. INPE is Brazil's main civilian research center for aerospace activities.

**Research Areas:**
- Space and Atmospheric Sciences
- Space Engineering and Technology
- Earth Observation by Satellites
- Meteorology and Climate Studies
- Heliophysics and Planetary Sciences
- Aeronomy and Upper Atmosphere Physics

**Major Programs:**
- **MESA** - Meteorological satellite image reception
- **SERE** - Satellite remote sensing
- **CBERS** - China-Brazil Earth Resources Satellite program
- **AMZ** - Amazon Research Program
- **CPTEC** - Weather Forecast and Climate Studies Center

**Notable Directors:**
- Fernando de Mendonça (first director, 1971-1976)
- Ricardo Galvão (2016-2019, fired after defending scientific data)

**Satellites:**
- SCD-1 (1993) - First Brazilian satellite
- SCD-2 (1998)
- CBERS series (China-Brazil partnership)

---

### 3.3 Instituto de Matemática Pura e Aplicada (IMPA)
**Institute of Pure and Applied Mathematics**

**Founded:** 1952 (first research unit established by CNPq)

**Location:** Rio de Janeiro, RJ (Jardim Botânico)

**Relation to Physics:**
While primarily a mathematics institute, IMPA has strong connections to mathematical physics, including:
- General relativity and differential geometry
- Statistical mechanics
- Dynamical systems
- Quantum field theory (mathematical aspects)

**Notable:**
- IMPA is world-renowned for mathematics research
- Hosts the Mathematical Physics group
- Publishes important works in mathematical physics

## 4. Major Research Facilities

### 4.1 Sirius - Brazilian Synchrotron Light Laboratory (LNLS)

**Founded:** 1997

**Location:** Campinas, SP (Barão Geraldo, adjacent to Unicamp)

**Operator:** CNPEM (Brazilian Center for Research in Energy and Materials)

**Funding:** CNPq and Ministry of Science and Technology

**Overview:**
Sirius is a 4th-generation synchrotron light source, one of the first of its kind globally and the **only synchrotron light source in Latin America**. It was designed and built entirely in Brazil by a team of Brazilian physicists, technicians, and engineers.

**Technical Specifications:**
- Energy: 3 GeV
- Storage ring circumference: 518.4 meters
- First 4th-generation synchrotron in the Southern Hemisphere

**Beamlines (Current and Planned):**
| Beamline | Technique | Energy Range |
|----------|-----------|--------------|
| Carnaúba | X-Ray Nanoscopy | - |
| Cateretê | Coherent and Time-resolved X-ray Scattering | - |
| Ema | X-ray Spectroscopy and Diffraction in Extreme Conditions | - |
| Imbuia | Infrared Micro and Nanospectroscopy | - |
| Ipê | Resonant Inelastic X-ray Scattering and Photoelectron Spectroscopy | - |
| Manacá | Macromolecular Micro and Nanocrystallography | - |

**Future:**
- Initial plan: 13 beamlines
- Final goal: 40 beamlines
- Energy range: 10 eV to 100 keV

**Inauguration:** 2018

**Research Applications:**
- Condensed matter physics
- Materials science
- Structural biology
- Chemistry
- Nanotechnology
- Earth and environmental sciences

---

### 4.2 TCABR Tokamak

**Location:** University of São Paulo, Plasma Physics Laboratory (LFP)

**Overview:**
The TCABR (Tokamak-Chauffage Alfvén de Brasil) is a medium-size tokamak device used for plasma physics and fusion research. It is one of the most important fusion research facilities in Latin America.

**Features:**
- Major radius: 0.61 m
- Minor radius: 0.18 m
- Toroidal magnetic field: up to 1.1 T
- Plasma current: up to 100 kA

**Research Focus:**
- Magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) instabilities
- Plasma-wall interactions
- Radio frequency heating
- Edge plasma physics
- Alfven wave heating

---

### 4.3 TBR-1 (Decommissioned)

**Historical Note:**
TBR-1 was the first tokamak built in Latin America, constructed at USP in 1981 under the leadership of Ricardo Galvão and Ivan Nascimento. It operated until 1995 and was instrumental in training a generation of Brazilian plasma physicists.

## 5. Notable Physicists

### Founding Generation ("Heroic Era" 1930s-1950s)

#### César Lattes (1924-2005)
**Most Famous Brazilian Physicist**

**Major Achievement:** Co-discoverer of the **pion** (pi-meson) in 1947

**Career:**
- Studied at University of São Paulo under Gleb Wataghin and Giuseppe Occhialini
- Worked at University of Bristol with Cecil Powell and Occhialini
- Discovered the pion using photographic plates exposed to cosmic rays at Chacaltaya mountain (Bolivia)
- Later produced pions artificially at UC Berkeley cyclotron (1948)
- Founded CBPF in 1949
- Helped establish Unicamp (1967)

**Legacy:**
- The Brazilian national science database is named "Lattes Platform" (Plataforma Lattes)
- Only Brazilian physicist with an entry in Encyclopædia Britannica
- TWAS Prize (1987)
- Despite being first author on the Nature paper announcing the pion, only Cecil Powell received the 1950 Nobel Prize (Nobel policy at the time awarded only group leaders)

#### José Leite Lopes (1918-2006)
**Theoretical Physicist**

**Major Achievement:** Predicted the existence of **Z⁰ bosons** (1958)

**Career:**
- Co-founder of CBPF (1949)
- Studied under Wolfgang Pauli at Princeton
- Worked with Einstein, von Neumann, and other giants at IAS
- Exiled during military dictatorship (1969), returned 1986
- Director of CBPF (1986)
- President of Brazilian Physical Society (1967-1971)

**Legacy:**
- UNESCO Science Prize (1999)
- Great Cross of the Brazilian Order of Scientific Merit
- His work on Z⁰ bosons influenced Weinberg, Glashow, and Salam's electroweak unification (Nobel Prize 1979)

#### Mário Schenberg (1914-1990)
**Theoretical Physicist and Astrophysicist**

**Major Achievements:**
- **Urca process** - Named the neutrino cooling process in supernovae (with George Gamow)
- **Schönberg-Chandrasekhar limit** - Maximum mass of stellar core (1942, with Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar)

**Career:**
- Professor at University of São Paulo
- Worked with David Bohm during Bohm's Brazilian exile
- Developed geometric algebras for quantum physics
- President of Brazilian Physical Society (1979-1981)

**Legacy:**
- The Mario Schenberg Graviton (gravitational wave detector) is named after him

#### Jayme Tiomno (1920-2011)
**Experimental and Theoretical Physicist**

**Major Contributions:**
- Co-founder of CBPF (1949)
- Founder of Brazilian Physical Society
- Work with John Wheeler on muon decay (1949)
- Research on general relativity and Gödel-type spacetimes

**Legacy:**
- Member of Brazilian Academy of Sciences
- Great Cross of the National Order of Scientific Merit

#### Gleb Wataghin (1899-1986)
**Russian-Italian-Brazilian Physicist**

**Role:** Father of modern physics in Brazil

**Career:**
- Came to Brazil in 1934 to organize the Department of Physics at the newly founded University of São Paulo
- Stayed until 1948
- Mentored: César Lattes, Oscar Sala, Mário Schenberg, Roberto Salmeron, Marcelo Damy de Souza Santos, Jayme Tiomno
- Returned to Italy (University of Turin) in 1949

**Legacy:**
- Unicamp's Institute of Physics named after him
- Established the tradition of physics research in Brazil

#### Oscar Sala (1922-2010)
**Nuclear Physicist**

**Career:**
- First President of Brazilian Physical Society (1966-1967)
- Scientific Director of FAPESP (1959-1965)
- President of SBPC (Brazilian Society for the Advancement of Science)
- Member of Brazilian Academy of Sciences and American Academy of Arts and Sciences
- Built USP's Pelletron accelerator (first in Latin America)

### Contemporary Physicists

## 6. Physics Research Areas of Strength

### 6.1 Condensed Matter Physics
Brazil has a long tradition of excellence in condensed matter physics, with strong experimental and theoretical groups at:
- USP
- Unicamp  
- UFRJ
- CBPF
- UFRGS

**Key Topics:**
- Superconductivity (high-temperature and conventional)
- Magnetism and magnetic materials
- Nanophysics and nanomaterials
- Strongly correlated electron systems
- Semiconductors and devices

### 6.2 Elementary Particle Physics
Active participation in major international experiments, particularly at CERN.

**Key Topics:**
- LHC physics (ATLAS, CMS, ALICE, LHCb)
- Neutrino physics
- Phenomenology of beyond Standard Model physics
- Heavy ion physics

### 6.3 Plasma Physics and Nuclear Fusion
Strong tradition since the construction of Brazil's first tokamak in 1981.

**Key Topics:**
- Tokamak physics
- Plasma confinement and stability
- Radio frequency heating
- Magnetic fusion energy research

### 6.4 Cosmology, Astrophysics and Gravitation
Notable contributions from theoretical and observational groups.

**Key Topics:**
- General relativity
- Cosmological models
- Black hole physics
- Gravitational waves
- Cosmic rays (legacy of Lattes)

### 6.5 Statistical Physics and Complex Systems
Strong mathematical physics tradition.

**Key Topics:**
- Phase transitions and critical phenomena
- Non-equilibrium statistical mechanics
- Complex networks
- Computational physics

### 6.6 Optics and Photonics
Growing research area with applications.

**Key Topics:**
- Nonlinear optics
- Quantum optics
- Photonic materials
- Biophotonics

### 6.7 Biological and Medical Physics
Emerging interdisciplinary field.

**Key Topics:**
- Medical imaging
- Biophysics of membranes
- Radiation biology
- Neurophysics

### 6.8 Applied Physics and Materials Science
Strong connections to industry and technology.

**Key Topics:**
- Semiconductors
- Nanotechnology
- Energy materials
- Environmental physics

## 7. International Collaborations

### 7.1 CERN - European Organization for Nuclear Research

**Status:** Brazil became the **first Latin American Associate Member State of CERN** in March 2024.

**History of Collaboration:**
- Formal cooperation began in 1990 with an International Cooperation Agreement
- Brazilian institutes participate in all four major LHC experiments:
  - **ATLAS**
  - **CMS**
  - **ALICE**
  - **LHCb**

**Contributions:**
- Detector construction and upgrades
- Data analysis
- Computing infrastructure (grid computing)
- Theoretical physics

**Institutions Involved:**
- USP
- Unicamp
- UFRJ
- CBPF
- UERJ (Rio de Janeiro State University)

### 7.2 ICTP - International Centre for Theoretical Physics

**ICTP-SAIFR** (South American Institute for Fundamental Research)
- Located at IFT-UNESP in São Paulo
- Partnership between ICTP, UNESP, and FAPESP
- Promotes high-energy physics, cosmology, and condensed matter physics in South America
- Hosts international schools and workshops

### 7.3 IAEA - International Atomic Energy Agency
Collaboration in nuclear physics and nuclear applications.

### 7.4 INPE International Collaborations
- **NASA** - Various space and Earth observation programs
- **ESA** (European Space Agency) - Space research
- **CNSA** (China National Space Administration) - CBERS program
- Space weather monitoring

### 7.5 Synchrotron Light Sources
- **LNLS** collaborates with synchrotron facilities worldwide
- Participation in SESAME (Middle East synchrotron)
- Collaboration with ESRF (European Synchrotron Radiation Facility), APS (Advanced Photon Source), and others

### 7.6 Other Major Collaborations
- **Gravitational Wave Detection:** Participation in international gravitational wave research
- **Neutrino Physics:** Collaboration with international neutrino experiments
- **Fusion Research:** Collaboration with ITER and other fusion programs

## Summary

Brazil has established itself as a major player in international physics research, particularly within Latin America. From the pioneering work of the "heroic generation" (Lattes, Leite Lopes, Schenberg, Tiomno) to contemporary participation in major international collaborations like CERN, Brazilian physicists have made significant contributions to our understanding of the physical world.

Key strengths include:
- Strong tradition in particle physics and cosmic ray research
- World-class synchrotron facility (Sirius/LNLS)
- Active participation in CERN experiments
- Excellent graduate programs in physics
- Growing international reputation in condensed matter and materials science
- Robust institutional framework with dedicated funding agencies

The physics community in Brazil continues to grow and develop, with increasing international collaborations and contributions to global scientific endeavors.

# Physics Institutions and Research in Indonesia

## Overview

Indonesia's physics research ecosystem has undergone significant transformation in recent years with the establishment of the National Research and Innovation Agency (BRIN) in 2021, which consolidated multiple research institutions including the Indonesian Institute of Sciences (LIPI), the National Nuclear Energy Agency (BATAN), the Agency for Assessment and Application of Technology (BPPT), and the National Institute of Aeronautics and Space (LAPAN). The country has a growing physics community with strong university programs, emerging international collaborations, and increasing focus on applied physics research aligned with national development priorities.

## 1. National Physics Societies

### Indonesian Physical Society (HFI - Himpunan Fisika Indonesia)
- **Founded**: 1976
- **Headquarters**: Various university chapters across Indonesia
- **Purpose**: Promote physics education, research, and professional development
- **Activities**: 
  - Organizes national physics conferences (Konferensi Nasional Fisika)
  - Physics Olympiad programs for students
  - Professional development workshops
  - Publications and scientific communication

### Indonesian Nuclear Society (Hatin - Himpunan Ahli Teknik Nuklir Indonesia)
- **Focus**: Nuclear science and engineering professionals
- **Affiliation**: Works closely with BRIN's Nuclear Energy Research Organization
- **Activities**: Conferences, technical workshops, nuclear safety education

### Indonesian Astronomical Society
- **Focus**: Promotes astronomy and astrophysics research
- **Activities**: Public outreach, amateur astronomy programs, collaboration with professional observatories

### Association of Indonesian Physicists in Higher Education
- **Focus**: University physics education and curriculum development
- **Activities**: Teacher training, educational research, accreditation support

### Bandung Institute of Technology (ITB)

**Department of Physics, Faculty of Mathematics and Natural Sciences**
- **Founded**: 1948 (oldest physics program in Indonesia)
- **Location**: Bandung, West Java
- **Faculty**: 65+ teaching staff organized into 5 research divisions
- **Students**: ~400 undergraduates, ~100 master's students, ~100 doctoral students

**Programs**:
- Undergraduate Physics (Sarjana Fisika) - ASIIN Accredited, BAN-PT "Unggul"
- Master in Physics (Magister Fisika) - BAN-PT "A"
- Master in Physics Education (Magister Pengajaran Fisika) - BAN-PT "B"
- Doctor in Physics (Doktor Fisika) - BAN-PT "A"
- Master in Nuclear Science and Engineering (established 2019)
- Doctor in Nuclear Engineering (established 2019)

**Research Divisions**:
1. **High Energy Theoretical Physics (FTETi)** - Quantum gravity, particle physics, cosmology, string theory
2. **Advanced Materials Physics and Technology** - Material synthesis, characterization, applications
3. **Nuclear Physics and Biophysics** - Molecular biophysics, radiation biophysics, medical physics
4. **Magnetic and Photonic Physics** - Non-linear optical materials, photonic technology, sensors
5. **Earth Physics and Complex Systems** - Geophysical observation, modeling, complex systems
6. **Instrumentation and Computational Physics** - IoT-based instrumentation, control systems

**Historical Note**: ITB Physics is the oldest physics program in Indonesia, established in 1948 as part of the Faculteit van Exacte Wetenschap.

**Website**: https://fi.itb.ac.id

## 3. National Research Centers

### National Research and Innovation Agency (BRIN)

**Badan Riset dan Inovasi Nasional**
- **Established**: May 5, 2021 (Presidential Regulation No. 33/2021)
- **Operational**: September 1, 2021
- **Status**: Government institution integrating LIPI, BATAN, BPPT, LAPAN

**Structure**: 12 Research Organizations (OR), including:

#### Research Organization for Nuclear Energy (ORTN)
- **Formerly**: BATAN (Badan Tenaga Nuklir Nasional)
- **Established**: September 1, 2021 (integration of BATAN into BRIN)
- **Current Head**: Dr. Syaiful Bakhri (appointed July 5, 2024)
- **Locations**: Serpong (Banten), Bandung (West Java), Yogyakarta (Central Java)

**Research Centers**:
1. **Center for Accelerator Technology Research (PRTA)**
   - DECY-13 cyclotron (13 MeV proton accelerator)
   - Medical radioisotope production for PET imaging
   - Linear accelerator (LINAC) systems development

2. **Center for Radiation Process Technology Research (PRTPR)**
   - Industrial radiation applications
   - Food irradiation and preservation
   - Material modification

3. **Center for Nuclear Reactor Technology Research (PRTRN)**
   - Reactor design and safety analysis
   - PeLUIt-40 HTGR (High-Temperature Gas-cooled Reactor) development
   - Small Modular Reactor (SMR) research

4. **Center for Radioisotopes, Radiopharmaceuticals, and Biodosimetry Technology Research (PRTRRB)**
   - Medical isotope production (Phosphorus-32, etc.)
   - Radiopharmaceutical development
   - Cancer therapy applications

5. **Center for Nuclear Safety, Metrology, and Quality Technology Research (PRTKMMN)**
   - Dosimetry standards and calibration
   - Risk assessment protocols
   - Regulatory support

6. **Center for Nuclear Materials and Radioactive Waste Technology Research (PRTBNLR)**
   - Nuclear materials engineering
   - Waste immobilization (vitrification, cementation)
   - Decommissioning protocols

7. **Center for Nuclear Beam Analysis Technology Research (PRTABN)**
   - Material characterization
   - Non-destructive testing
   - Environmental monitoring

**Research Reactors**:
| Reactor | Location | Type | Power | Status |
|---------|----------|------|-------|--------|
| TRIGA 2000 | Bandung | TRIGA Mark II | 2 MW | Operational since 1964 |
| Kartini | Yogyakarta | TRIGA Mark II | 100 kW | Operational since 1979 |
| G.A. Siwabessy (RSG-GAS) | Serpong | Multipurpose | 30 MW | Southeast Asia's largest research reactor |

**Key Programs (2025-2029)**:
- Nuclear reactor energy applications
- Nuclear fuel cycle development
- Radioisotope and radiopharmaceutical production
- Nuclear technology for food security
- Small Modular Reactor (SMR) deployment targeting 250 MW by 2029

**Website**: https://www.brin.go.id

## 4. Major Research Facilities

### Research Reactors

#### G.A. Siwabessy Multipurpose Reactor (RSG-GAS)
- **Location**: PUSPIPTEK Complex, Serpong
- **Type**: Open pool-type multipurpose reactor
- **Power**: 30 MW thermal
- **Commissioned**: 1987
- **Significance**: Southeast Asia's largest research reactor
- **Applications**:
  - Radioisotope production
  - Neutron activation analysis
  - Neutron scattering research
  - Material testing
  - Nuclear training

#### TRIGA 2000 Reactor
- **Location**: Bandung
- **Type**: TRIGA Mark II pool-type
- **Power**: 2 MW
- **First Criticality**: 1964 (upgraded to 2 MW in 2000)
- **Applications**: Training, basic research, neutron activation analysis

#### Kartini Reactor
- **Location**: Yogyakarta
- **Type**: TRIGA Mark II
- **Power**: 100 kW
- **First Criticality**: 1979
- **Significance**: Fully Indonesian-engineered design
- **Applications**: Education, training, basic nuclear physics research

---

### Accelerators

#### DECY-13 Cyclotron
- **Location**: Serpong
- **Type**: Compact cyclotron
- **Energy**: 13 MeV proton
- **Achievement**: Stable 20 nA beam current achieved (December 2024)
- **Applications**: Medical radioisotope production for PET imaging

#### Linear Accelerators (LINAC)
- **Applications**: Industrial irradiation, food processing, material modification
- **Development**: S-band standing-wave systems

---

### Specialized Laboratories

#### Neutron Scattering Laboratory
- **Location**: Serpong
- **Facility**: Utilizes RSG-GAS neutron beams
- **Applications**: Material structure analysis, condensed matter physics

#### Radioisotope and Radiopharmaceutical Production Facility
- **Location**: Serpong
- **Established**: 1989
- **Products**: Mo-99, I-131, P-32, and other medical isotopes

#### Hot Cell Facilities
- **Location**: Serpong, Bandung
- **Purpose**: Remote handling of high-activity radioactive materials
- **Applications**: Isotope processing, radiopharmaceutical preparation

## Conclusion

Indonesia's physics ecosystem is in a transformative phase following the establishment of BRIN in 2021, which consolidated major research institutions. The country maintains strong physics education at its top universities (UI, ITB, UGM, IPB) and has significant nuclear research capabilities through ORTN-BRIN. Key strengths include:

1. **Nuclear Infrastructure**: Three operational research reactors and growing capabilities in radioisotope production
2. **University Programs**: Established physics programs with increasing international accreditation
3. **Applied Physics Focus**: Strong emphasis on applications in energy, health, agriculture, and environment
4. **International Engagement**: Active participation in IAEA programs, regional cooperation, and emerging CERN collaboration

Challenges include:
- Transition and integration following the BRIN consolidation
- Need for increased research funding
- Development of advanced research facilities beyond nuclear physics
- Building stronger international research partnerships

The future outlook is positive with planned development of Small Modular Reactors, growing investment in quantum technology research, and expanding international collaborations. Indonesia's physics community is positioned to make greater contributions to regional and global scientific advancement.

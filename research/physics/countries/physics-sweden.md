# Physics in Sweden: A Comprehensive Overview

## Executive Summary

Sweden has a distinguished history in physics research, with four Nobel laureates in Physics and world-leading research facilities. The country hosts two of Europe's most significant research infrastructures—MAX IV synchrotron and the European Spallation Source (ESS)—making Lund a global hub for materials science research. Swedish physics excels in areas including quantum technology, photonics, plasma physics, condensed matter physics, and subatomic physics, with strong international collaborations particularly with CERN.

---

## 1. National Physics Societies

### Svenska Fysikersamfundet (Swedish Physical Society)

The Swedish Physical Society is the professional organization representing physicists in Sweden. Founded in 1911, the Society aims to:

- Promote the development of physics research and education in Sweden
- Facilitate collaboration among physicists across different subdisciplines
- Represent Swedish physics in international forums
- Organize conferences, seminars, and public outreach activities
- Support physics education at all levels

The Society works closely with universities, research institutes, and government agencies to advance physics research and its applications in Swedish society.

### International Union of Pure and Applied Physics (IUPAP)

Swedish physicists have played prominent roles in IUPAP. Notably, Kai Siegbahn (Nobel laureate) served as president of IUPAP from 1981 to 1984, strengthening Sweden's international standing in the physics community.

## 2. Major University Physics Departments

### Uppsala University

**Department of Physics and Astronomy**

Uppsala University, founded in 1477, is the oldest university in Scandinavia and home to one of Sweden's most prestigious physics departments. The Ångström Laboratory serves as the primary research facility.

**Key Research Areas:**
- Materials physics and nanotechnology
- Quantum materials
- Photon physics
- Theoretical physics
- Space physics
- Nuclear physics

**Notable Historical Connection:**
- Manne Siegbahn held the professorship in experimental physics at Uppsala (1922-1937)
- Kai Siegbahn held the same chair (1954-1984)
- The Siegbahn tradition of X-ray spectroscopy continues to influence research

**Key Facilities:**
- Ångström Laboratory (Ångströmlaboratoriet)
- Advanced electron microscopy facilities
- Clean room facilities for nanofabrication

### Lund University

**Department of Physics**

Lund hosts two of the world's most advanced research facilities—MAX IV and ESS—making it a global center for materials research. The physics department has strong ties to these facilities.

**Key Research Areas:**
- Synchrotron radiation physics
- Nuclear physics
- Particle physics
- Solid state physics
- Attosecond physics and ultrafast optics
- Biophysics and medical physics
- NanoLund (nanoscience center)

**Notable Achievement:**
- Professor Anne L'Huillier received the 2023 Nobel Prize in Physics for her pioneering work on attosecond pulses of light

**Key Facilities:**
- MAX IV Laboratory access
- European Spallation Source (ESS) proximity
- NanoLund research center

### Stockholm University

**Department of Physics**

Located at the AlbaNova University Centre, Stockholm University's physics department is internationally recognized for theoretical and experimental physics.

**Key Research Areas:**
- Quantum physics and quantum technology
- Photonics and quantum optics
- Condensed matter physics
- Particle physics and astroparticle physics
- Medical radiation physics

**Notable Features:**
- Co-location with Nordita (Nordic Institute for Theoretical Physics)
- Strong theoretical physics tradition
- 50-100 researchers working in quantum physics
- Nobel laureate Frank Wilczek holds a position at Stockholm University

### KTH Royal Institute of Technology (Stockholm)

**Department of Physics**

KTH is Sweden's largest technical university and combines fundamental physics research with engineering applications.

**Key Research Areas:**
- Quantum materials and nanophysics
- High-energy physics (CERN collaborations)
- Nuclear physics and fusion research
- Medical imaging and radiation physics
- Computational physics

**Leadership:**
- Head of Department: Jens Bardarson
- Located at AlbaNova University Centre, co-located with Stockholm University physics

### Chalmers University of Technology (Gothenburg)

**Department of Physics**

Chalmers physics department bridges fundamental research with industrial applications, particularly in materials science and energy technology.

**Research Divisions:**
1. **Chemical Physics** – Surface and nanoscience, sustainable energy systems
2. **Condensed Matter and Materials Theory** – Materials modeling and design
3. **Materials Physics** – Fundamental phenomena to applications
4. **Microstructure Physics** – Industrial materials development
5. **Nano and Biophysics** – Nanoscopic and biomolecular structures
6. **Subatomic, High Energy and Plasma Physics** – Fundamental physics and fusion

**Key Facilities:**
- Chalmers Material Analysis Laboratory (CMAL)
- Chalmers e-commons
- Leads the Graphene Flagship, one of Europe's largest research initiatives
- Formulaex research center

### Other Notable Institutions

**Linköping University**
- Strong in materials physics and semiconductor physics
- Collaboration partner in quantum technology initiatives
- Expertise in 2D materials and quantum materials

**Umeå University**
- Condensed matter physics
- Physics education research
- Strong in statistical physics and complex systems

**University of Gothenburg**
- Physics education and research
- Collaboration with Chalmers on physics programs

## 3. National Research Centers

**Overview:**

- Originally in Copenhagen, moved to Stockholm in 2007

**Mission:**

- Strengthen Nordic collaboration in theoretical physics
- Support graduate-level physics education

**Main Research Areas:**
- Astrophysics and cosmology

- Quantum information and networks

- Scientific conferences and workshops
- Postdoctoral programs

**Funding:**
- Nordic Council of Ministers
- Swedish Research Council
- Host universities (KTH and Stockholm University)

## 4. Major Research Facilities

### MAX IV Laboratory

**Overview:**
MAX IV is Sweden's national synchrotron radiation facility and one of the most advanced synchrotron light sources in the world.

**Key Facts:**
- **Location:** Lund, Sweden
- **Host:** Lund University
- **Opened:** 2016
- **Construction Cost:** Approximately SEK 6 billion (~€600 million)
- **Employees:** ~300 people
- **Annual Users:** ~1,900 researchers from around the world

**Technical Specifications:**
- Fourth-generation synchrotron light source
- World's first synchrotron designed with a multibend achromat lattice
- Light up to 100 times brighter than previous generation facilities
- Linear accelerator feeding two storage rings
- 16+ experimental stations (beamlines)

**Research Areas:**
- Materials science and nanotechnology
- Life sciences and structural biology
- Environmental science
- Cultural heritage preservation
- Energy research (batteries, solar cells)
- Quantum materials

**Funding Sources:**
- Swedish Research Council (Vetenskapsrådet)
- Vinnova
- Swedish universities
- Formas
- Knut and Alice Wallenberg Foundation
- Novo Nordisk Foundation

**Notable Achievements:**
- Over 900 published articles using MAX IV data since 2016
- More than 445 research projects in 2024 alone

### European Spallation Source (ESS)

**Overview:**
ESS is a pan-European research infrastructure under construction in Lund, Sweden. When operational, it will be the world's most powerful neutron source.

**Key Facts:**
- **Location:** Lund, Sweden
- **Construction Start:** 2014
- **Scientific User Program:** Scheduled for 2027
- **Estimated Cost:** €3.3 billion (2013 prices)
- **Staff:** ~500 people (full operation)
- **Annual Users:** ~3,000 researchers expected

**Technical Specifications:**
- Long-pulse spallation neutron source (unique worldwide)
- Proton beam energy: 2 GeV
- Target: Rotating helium-cooled tungsten wheel
- 15+ neutron instruments planned initially
- Linear accelerator with superconducting cavities
- Located adjacent to MAX IV for complementary research

**Neutron Instruments:**
- **SANS instruments:** SKADI, LoKI (small-angle neutron scattering)
- **Reflectometers:** FREIA, ESTIA
- **Diffractometers:** HEIMDAL, DREAM, BEER, MAGiC
- **Spectrometers:** CSPEC, T-REX, BIFROST, VESPA, MIRACLES
- **Imaging:** ODIN
- **Macromolecular:** NMX

**Member Countries (13):**
Czech Republic, Denmark, Estonia, France, Germany, Hungary, Italy, Norway, Poland, Spain, Sweden, Switzerland, United Kingdom

**Research Applications:**
- Materials science and magnetism
- Life sciences and drug development
- Energy materials (fuel cells, batteries)
- Engineering and industrial applications
- Environmental science
- Fundamental physics

**Sustainability:**
- Aims to be world's first completely sustainable large-scale research center
- CO2-neutral operations planned
- Collaboration with wind power projects

## 5. Notable Physicists and Nobel Laureates

### Swedish Nobel Laureates in Physics (4 total)

#### 1. Gustaf Dalén (1912)
- **Award:** "For his invention of automatic valves designed to be used in combination with gas accumulators in lighthouses and buoys"
- **Contribution:** Developed the Dalén light and sun valve (solar-powered automatic regulator for lighthouse lights)
- **Institution:** Swedish Gas Accumulator Company (AGA)

#### 2. Karl Manne Georg Siegbahn (1924)
- **Award:** "For his discoveries and research in the field of X-ray spectroscopy"
- **Born:** December 3, 1886, Örebro, Sweden
- **Died:** September 26, 1978, Stockholm
- **Key Contributions:**
  - Precision measurements of X-ray wavelengths
  - Developed Siegbahn notation for X-ray spectral lines
  - Improved X-ray spectrometer design
  - Provided crucial experimental data for quantum theory development
- **Institutions:** Lund University, Uppsala University, Nobel Institute
- **Legacy:** Founded the Manne Siegbahn Institute; established a scientific dynasty

#### 3. Hannes Alfvén (1970)
- **Award:** "For fundamental work and discoveries in magnetohydrodynamics with fruitful applications in different parts of plasma physics"
- **Born:** May 30, 1908, Norrköping, Sweden
- **Died:** April 2, 1995, Djursholm
- **Key Contributions:**
  - Founded the field of magnetohydrodynamics (MHD)
  - Discovered Alfvén waves (hydromagnetic plasma oscillations)
  - Developed theories of aurora formation
  - Predicted galactic magnetic fields (1937)
  - Explained Van Allen radiation belts
  - Worked on solar wind and plasma cosmology
- **Institutions:** University of Uppsala, KTH Royal Institute of Technology, University of California San Diego
- **Legacy:** The Hannes Alfvén Prize (European Physical Society); Alfvén Laboratory at KTH; asteroid 1778 Alfvén named in his honor

#### 4. Kai Manne Börje Siegbahn (1981)
- **Award:** "For his contribution to the development of high-resolution electron spectroscopy"
- **Born:** April 20, 1918, Lund, Sweden
- **Died:** July 20, 2007, Ängelholm
- **Key Contributions:**
  - Developed X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), also known as ESCA (Electron Spectroscopy for Chemical Analysis)
  - Enabled surface chemical analysis at atomic level
  - Revolutionized materials characterization
- **Institutions:** Royal Institute of Technology, Uppsala University
- **Legacy:** XPS/ESCA now standard technique in materials science; son of Manne Siegbahn, making them one of few parent-child Nobel laureate pairs

### Other Notable Swedish Physicists

#### Anne L'Huillier (Nobel Laureate 2023 - French but based at Lund University)
- Awarded for "experimental methods that generate attosecond pulses of light"
- Professor at Lund University since 1995
- Pioneer in attosecond physics

#### Johannes Rydberg (1854-1919)
- Known for the Rydberg formula in atomic spectroscopy
- Worked at Lund University
- The Rydberg constant is named after him

#### Oskar Klein (1894-1977)
- Known for the Klein-Gordon equation
- Kaluza-Klein theory (unification of gravity and electromagnetism)
- Worked at Stockholm University

#### Carl Wilhelm Oseen (1879-1944)
- Fluid dynamics and theoretical physics
- Director of the Nobel Institute for Theoretical Physics

#### Torsten Gustafson (1904-1988)
- Co-founder of Nordita with Niels Bohr
- Professor at Lund University

## 6. Physics Research Areas of Strength

### Quantum Technology and Photonics

Sweden has identified quantum technology as a strategic research area with significant investments:

**Key Strengths:**
- Attosecond physics (Lund University, Anne L'Huillier)
- Quantum optics and quantum information
- Superconducting quantum devices (Chalmers)
- Photonic quantum technologies
- Quantum sensors and communication

**Notable Initiative:**
- Stockholm University, Linköping University, and Uppsala University jointly applied for a Strategic Research Area (SFO) in Quantum Technology
- Knut and Alice Wallenberg Foundation has invested over 1 billion SEK in quantum research
- Chalmers has developed a world-class quantum computer

### Condensed Matter and Materials Physics

Sweden has a strong tradition in materials characterization and development:

**Key Areas:**
- X-ray and neutron scattering methods
- Surface and thin-film physics
- Magnetic materials and spintronics
- Nanomaterials and 2D materials (graphene, etc.)
- Battery and energy materials
- Quantum materials

### Plasma Physics

Following Hannes Alfvén's legacy:

**Key Areas:**
- Fusion plasma physics
- Space and astrophysical plasmas
- Magnetohydrodynamics
- Industrial plasma applications

### Particle and Astroparticle Physics

Strong involvement in international collaborations:

**Key Areas:**
- High-energy physics at CERN
- Neutrino physics
- Dark matter searches
- Astroparticle physics
- Cosmology

### Nuclear Physics

**Key Areas:**
- Nuclear structure and reactions
- Applied nuclear physics
- Medical applications
- Nuclear safety and safeguards

### Synchrotron and Neutron Science

Sweden's unique position with both MAX IV and ESS:

**Key Areas:**
- Synchrotron radiation methods development
- Neutron scattering techniques
- Materials characterization at large facilities
- Industrial applications

### Photonics

Sweden has over 200 photonics companies and strong academic research:

**Key Areas:**
- Fiber optics and communications
- Laser physics
- Biophotonics
- Optical sensors
- Silicon photonics

## 7. International Collaborations

### CERN (European Organization for Nuclear Research)

Sweden has been a member of CERN since 1951 and plays an active role:

**Swedish Involvement:**
- ATLAS experiment (proton-proton collisions)
- ALICE experiment (heavy-ion physics)
- CMS experiment
- ISOLDE (radioactive ion beams)
- Neutrino Platform

**Contributions:**
- Detector development and construction
- Physics analysis
- Computing and data processing
- Theory and phenomenology

**Notable Connection:**
- A street at CERN's Prévessin site in France is named "Route Siegbahn" in honor of Manne Siegbahn

### European Research Infrastructure Consortium (ERIC)

**ESS ERIC (European Spallation Source):**
- Sweden and Denmark are host countries
- 13 member countries
- Sweden provides significant portion of funding

### European Synchrotron Radiation Facility (ESRF)

Swedish researchers have access through Swedish Research Council funding, complementing domestic MAX IV access.

### ILL (Institut Laue-Langevin)

European neutron source in Grenoble, France—Swedish users transitioning to ESS when operational.

### European Space Agency (ESA)

Swedish participation in space physics missions:
- Space weather research
- Plasma physics experiments
- Satellite instrumentation

### Fusion Research

**EUROfusion Consortium:**
- Sweden participates in European fusion research
- Chalmers and KTH involved in plasma physics for fusion
- Alcator C-Mod (MIT) collaborations
- Wendelstein 7-X (Germany) involvement

### International Research Collaborations

**Bilateral Agreements:**
- Nordic collaboration through Nordita and NordForsk
- German-Swedish collaborations in materials science
- US collaborations (Berkeley Lab, Argonne National Lab)
- Asian partnerships (Japan, China) in photonics and materials

### EU Research Programs

Swedish physics research is well-represented in:
- Horizon Europe
- European Research Council (ERC) grants
- Marie Skłodowska-Curie Actions
- Quantum Flagship
- Photonics21
- Graphene Flagship (Chalmers leads)

## 10. Summary and Future Outlook

Sweden maintains a strong position in international physics research through:

1. **World-Class Facilities:** Hosting MAX IV and ESS places Sweden at the forefront of materials science research
2. **Nobel Tradition:** Four physics laureates and continued excellence
3. **Strong Theoretical Base:** Nordita as a hub for Nordic theoretical physics
4. **International Integration:** Active participation in CERN and EU research programs
5. **Strategic Focus:** Quantum technology and photonics identified as priority areas
6. **Industrial Connection:** Strong links between academic research and industry, especially in photonics and materials

**Emerging Opportunities:**
- Quantum technology development
- Integration of MAX IV and ESS for complementary research
- Expansion of quantum computing capabilities at Chalmers
- Growth in quantum sensing and communication applications

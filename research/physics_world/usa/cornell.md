## History

The Cornell University Department of Physics has a rich history dating back to the university's founding in 1868. The department has been at the forefront of numerous groundbreaking discoveries and has shaped modern physics in fundamental ways.

### Historical Milestones

- **1868**: Department established with Cornell University's founding
- **1875**: Professor William A. Anthony and George S. Moler built an early dynamo (electric generator), which was later exhibited at the Century of Progress International Exposition in Chicago (1933)
- **1935**: Cornell built one of the first cyclotrons
- **1972**: Discovery of superfluid helium-3 by David M. Lee, Robert C. Richardson, and Douglas D. Osheroff, leading to the **1996 Nobel Prize in Physics**
- **1979**: Completion of the Cornell Electron Storage Ring (CESR), a 768-meter electron-positron collider
- **1980**: Establishment of the Cornell High Energy Synchrotron Source (CHESS)
- **2006**: Formation of CLASSE (Cornell Laboratory for Accelerator-based ScienceS and Education) through merger of CHESS and LEPP
- **2012**: Cornell researchers played key roles in the Higgs boson discovery at CERN's LHC
- **2024**: LHC collaborations (including Cornell's CMS team) awarded the **Breakthrough Prize in Fundamental Physics**

### Nobel Laureates Associated with Cornell Physics

| Year | Laureate(s) | Achievement |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| 1996 | David M. Lee, Robert C. Richardson, Douglas D. Osheroff | Discovery of superfluid helium-3 |

### Historic Faculty

- **Edward L. Nichols** (1854-1937): Pioneering researcher in phosphorescence, fluorescence, and luminescence
- **Hans A. Bethe**: Nobel Laureate who spent much of his career at Cornell; the annual Bethe Lecture series honors his legacy
- **Paul Ginsparg**: Creator of arXiv, the transformative preprint server that revolutionized scientific communication
- **Robert R. Wilson**: First director of Fermilab, contributor to CESR design; Wilson Laboratory named in his honor

## Research Areas and Specializations

The department organizes research into eight major areas:

### 1. Accelerator Physics
Cornell has the **largest graduate program in accelerator physics in the United States**. Research includes:
- Operation of the Cornell Electron Storage Ring (CESR)
- Design of the Energy Recovery Linear accelerator (ERL)
- Development of superconducting radio-frequency (SRF) cavities
- Participation in the International Linear Collider (ILC) design
- NSF Center for Bright Beams (led by Cornell)

### 2. Astrophysics, General Relativity and Cosmology
- Modeling of phenomena in our solar system, galaxy, and early universe
- Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) research (CCAT-prime, Simons Observatory, CMB-S4)
- Gravitational wave astronomy (LIGO collaboration)
- Dark matter and dark energy studies
- Black hole physics and numerical relativity

### 3. Biological Physics
- Single-molecule biophysics
- Molecular motors and protein crystallography
- DNA packaging and gene expression
- Insect flight aerodynamics
- X-ray detectors for biological applications

### 4. Experimental Condensed Matter Physics
Research strengths include:
- **Nanostructure physics**: Carbon nanotubes, graphene, molecular electronics
- **Quantum materials**: High-temperature superconductors, topological materials, 2D materials
- **Spintronics**: Spin torque, spin transport in quantum materials
- **Soft condensed matter**: Complex fluids, colloids, polymer networks
- **Low-temperature physics**: Superfluid helium, supersolids
- **X-ray physics**: Time-dependent phenomena using high-speed detectors

### 5. Experimental Elementary Particle Physics
- Member of the **CMS collaboration** at CERN's Large Hadron Collider
- Higgs boson property measurements
- Search for physics beyond the Standard Model (dark matter, supersymmetry)
- Cornell leads the **$88 million CMS detector upgrade** for the High-Luminosity LHC
- Hardware construction for pixel detectors and track triggers

### 6. Physics Education Research (PER)
- Study of how students learn physics
- Development of innovative teaching methods
- Laboratory instruction improvement
- AI-assisted learning tools

### 7. Theoretical Condensed Matter Physics
- Quantum condensed matter (topological phases, quantum spin liquids)
- Strongly correlated electron systems
- Cold atomic gases
- Density functional theory
- Statistical mechanics and critical phenomena
- Computational physics methods

### 8. Theoretical Elementary Particle Physics
- Physics beyond the Standard Model
- String theory and holography
- Lattice gauge theories
- Particle astrophysics and cosmology
- Conformal field theories

## Major Research Centers and Facilities

### CLASSE - Cornell Laboratory for Accelerator-based ScienceS and Education

CLASSE is a federation of research centers formed in 2006, housed in Wilson Laboratory and Newman Laboratory.

**Website**: [classecornell.edu](https://www.classe.cornell.edu)

#### CESR - Cornell Electron Storage Ring
- **768-meter** electron-positron collider buried 40 feet beneath campus athletic fields
- Operating energy range: 3.5–12 GeV center-of-mass
- Completed in 1979
- Used for accelerator physics R&D and as a test-bed for Linear Collider concepts
- Operated as the world's highest luminosity electron-positron collider in the 1990s

#### CHESS - Cornell High Energy Synchrotron Source
- NSF-funded national user facility
- High-intensity X-ray source for multidisciplinary research
- Focus on protein crystallography (NIH support)
- Beamlines available to national and international researchers

#### ERL - Energy Recovery Linear Accelerator
- Innovative new X-ray light source under development
- First demonstration of energy-recovering accelerators (concept proposed at Cornell in 1965)
- Enables time-resolved X-ray studies and coherent diffraction

### Center for Bright Beams (CBB)

- **NSF Science and Technology Center** led by Cornell
- $22.5 million grant renewal (2021)
- Mission: Increase intensity of charged particle beams while decreasing costs
- Collaboration of 8 universities and 3 national labs

### Laboratory of Atomic and Solid State Physics (LASSP)

A multidisciplinary center focused on:
- Condensed matter physics
- Biological physics
- Optics and quantum information
- Houses the Student Machine Shop for hands-on fabrication training

### Cornell NanoScale Science & Technology Facility (CNF)

- National Nanotechnology Coordinated Infrastructure (NNCI) member
- Core user facility for micro- and nanofabrication
- Enables "top-down" lithography (<10 nm resolution) and "bottom-up" guided assembly

### Kavli Institute at Cornell

- Devoted to next-generation nanoscale tools and methodologies
- Funds cross-cutting research teams in nanoscale imaging, manipulation, and control

### Other Research Centers

## Recent Achievements and Breakthroughs (2023-2025)

### 2025

- **Breakthrough Prize for LHC Collaborations**: Cornell celebrated as CMS collaborators shared in the 2024 Breakthrough Prize in Fundamental Physics
- **Nobel Prize Connection**: Michel H. Devoret (A.D. White Professor-at-Large at Cornell) shared the 2025 Nobel Prize in Physics for macroscopic quantum mechanical tunneling
- **Google Quantum AI Collaboration**: First observation of non-Abelian anyons using protocols developed by Eun-Ah Kim and collaborators
- **3D-Printed Superconductor**: Record-performance superconductors achieved via one-step 3D printing method

### 2024

- **CMS Detector Upgrade**: Cornell coordinating $88 million upgrade to the CMS detector for the High-Luminosity LHC (2030)
- **Bragg Glass Discovery**: Elusive phase of matter observed using machine learning analysis (published in *Nature Physics*)
- **APS Fellows**: Thomas Hartman and Gregory Fuchs elected to American Physical Society
- **New Nobel Laureates**: Two Cornell alumni returned as Nobel laureates

### 2023

- **Ultrafast Electron Diffraction**: Captured light-triggered twisting motion in atomically thin materials
- **IRIS-HEP Software Institute**: $25 million NSF funding for next-generation HEP software
- **NIH Funding**: $17.4 million awarded for CHESS subfacility for structural biology
- **Topological Quantum Computing**: Protocol development for braiding non-Abelian anyons

## Academic Programs

### Undergraduate Program

**Degree Offered**: Bachelor of Arts (BA) in Physics

#### Major Options

1. **Inside Concentration** (68 credits minimum)
   - Principal path to graduate work in physics
   - Core physics courses plus electives within the department
   - Rigorous preparation for research careers

2. **Outside Concentration** (62 credits minimum)
   - Flexibility to combine physics with other interests
   - Popular concentrations: Biology (premed), Economics, Computer Science, Education

#### Core Requirements
- Three-semester introductory physics sequence
- Special relativity
- Quantum Physics I & II
- Advanced laboratory courses
- Intermediate mechanics and electromagnetism

#### Honors and Senior Thesis
- **Honors in Physics**: GPA 3.7+ and senior thesis with A- or better, or faculty nomination
- **Senior Thesis**: Two-semester research project (PHYS 4498/4499) culminating in written thesis and defense
- Research encouraged to begin by junior year

#### Undergraduate Awards
| Award | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Yennie Prize** | Outstanding senior for future physics research |
| **Kieval Prize** | Senior demonstrating research promise |
| **Hartman Prize** | Outstanding experimental physics work |
| **Erik Cassel '90 Prize** | Exceptional programming in physics |

### Graduate Program

**Degree Offered**: Ph.D. in Physics (no terminal Master's)

#### Research Concentrations

| Area | Research Topics |
|------|-----------------|
| **Theoretical Condensed Matter** | Quantum phases, topological materials, cold atoms, statistical mechanics |
| **Experimental Condensed Matter** | Quantum materials, nanoscience, biophysics, x-ray physics |

## Unique Features and Facilities

### On-Campus Accelerator
Cornell is one of the few universities worldwide with a major particle accelerator (CESR) on campus, providing unique hands-on training opportunities for students.

### arXiv
The department is home to **arXiv**, the revolutionary preprint server created by Paul Ginsparg in 1991, transforming scientific communication across physics and related fields.

### Student Machine Shop
LASSP operates a fully equipped machine shop where students learn fabrication skills, supporting projects from physics to architecture and engineering.

### Bethe Lectures
Annual lecture series bringing world-renowned physicists to Cornell, honoring Hans Bethe's legacy. Recent lecturers have included Nobel laureates and leaders in quantum science.

### Research Opportunities for Undergraduates
- **Nexus Scholars Program**: Summer research with funding
- **Independent Study (PHYS 4490)**: Research for credit
- **REU Programs**: NSF Research Experiences for Undergraduates
- Access to world-class facilities: CHESS, CNF, LASSP

### Collaborative Environment
The department emphasizes a congenial, collaborative atmosphere where:
- Graduate students work closely with faculty
- Undergraduates participate in cutting-edge research
- Theory and experimental groups interact regularly
- Cross-disciplinary work is encouraged

## Summary

Cornell University's Department of Physics stands as one of the premier physics departments in the United States, distinguished by:

1. **Historic excellence**: 150+ years of contributions to physics, including the Nobel Prize-winning discovery of superfluid helium-3
2. **Unique facilities**: On-campus accelerator (CESR), synchrotron light source (CHESS), and leading nanofabrication facilities
3. **Breadth of research**: From the smallest particles to the largest cosmic structures
4. **Leadership in accelerator physics**: The largest U.S. graduate program and NSF Center for Bright Beams
5. **Innovation in scientific communication**: Home to arXiv
6. **Commitment to education**: Strong undergraduate and graduate programs with emphasis on research participation
7. **Recent breakthroughs**: Leading roles in LHC physics, quantum computing, and materials discovery

The department combines a tradition of fundamental discovery with cutting-edge modern research, providing students with unparalleled opportunities to contribute to the frontiers of physics.

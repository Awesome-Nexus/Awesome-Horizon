## üí° Study Tips from Experts

### From Nobel Laureates & Top Physicists

**Richard Feynman:**
- "What I cannot create, I do not understand"
- Work through problems yourself
- Teach concepts to others

**Gerard 't Hooft:**
- Do not believe anything on faith - check it
- Try alternative approaches
- Do all the exercises
- Reach the stage where you discover errors in texts

**General Advice:**
1. **Mathematics is the language** - Don't skimp on math preparation
2. **Work problems** - Physics is learned by doing, not reading
3. **Physical intuition** - Always ask "what does this mean physically?"
4. **Connect concepts** - Physics is interconnected, not isolated topics
5. **Use multiple resources** - Different explanations click for different people
6. **Join communities** - Physics Forums, StackExchange, study groups
7. **Do research early** - UROP, summer programs, independent projects

## üéØ Gerard 't Hooft's Guide to Becoming a Theoretical Physicist

Nobel Laureate Gerard 't Hooft has created an invaluable resource for aspiring theoretical physicists:

**[How to Become a Good Theoretical Physicist](https://www.goodtheorist.science/)**

### The Complete Curriculum According to 't Hooft:

**Primary Languages:**
- English (essential for reading papers)
- Mathematics (the language of physics)

**Foundation (Secondary School Level):**
- Basic arithmetic, algebra, geometry
- Calculus (differentiation, integration)
- Basic mechanics and electromagnetism

**Undergraduate Subjects:**
- Calculus (multivariable, vector calculus)
- Linear algebra
- Differential equations
- Classical mechanics (Newtonian, Lagrangian, Hamiltonian)
- Electromagnetism (Maxwell's equations)
- Thermodynamics and statistical mechanics
- Quantum mechanics (introduction)
- Special relativity

**Graduate Subjects:**
- Quantum mechanics (advanced)
- Quantum field theory
- General relativity
- Particle physics
- Condensed matter physics
- Advanced mathematical methods

**'t Hooft's Advice:**
1. Study at a university and absorb everything
2. Check everything - don't believe on faith
3. Try alternative approaches
4. Do all exercises
5. Reach the stage where you find errors in texts
6. Be prepared for at least 5 years of intense study
7. Intelligence, interest, and determination required

**Famous Quote:**
> "If you really want to contribute to our theoretical understanding of physical laws - and it is an exciting experience if you succeed! - there are many things you need to know. First of all, be serious about it."

## üìà Learning Path Timeline

### Year 1-2: Foundations (High School/Early Undergraduate)
- Complete Khan Academy physics
- Read conceptual physics textbooks
- Watch Walter Lewin's 8.01 lectures
- Practice basic problems
- Join physics club/olympiad

### Year 3-4: Core Undergraduate
- Complete MIT OCW 8.01, 8.02, 8.03
- Study calculus, linear algebra, differential equations
- Read Griffiths (Mechanics, E&M, QM)
- Join research group
- Attend physics summer school

### Year 5-6: Advanced Undergraduate/Graduate Prep
- Study graduate-level courses (Theoretical Minimum)
- Read advanced textbooks (Goldstein, Jackson, Shankar)
- Take Physics GRE
- Apply to graduate schools/fellowships
- Complete senior thesis

### Year 7+: Graduate School
- Core graduate courses (QFT, GR, Stat Mech)
- Choose specialization
- Pass qualifying exams
- Conduct original research
- Publish papers
- Attend conferences

### Post-PhD: Research Career
- Postdoctoral positions
- Fellowship applications
- Independent research
- Grant writing
- Teaching/mentoring
- Professional networking
